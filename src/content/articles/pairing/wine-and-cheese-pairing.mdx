Wine and cheese is one of those pairings that's been so thoroughly mythologized that the actual guidance has gotten lost. The "rules" you see most often are either too vague ("drink red with aged cheese") or too prescriptive ("only Sancerre with chèvre"). Neither helps you at an actual cheese board.

Here's what actually matters — and what doesn't.

## The one rule that always holds

High-acid wines pair with almost any cheese. Acidity cuts through fat and salt, refreshes the palate, and keeps the pairing from feeling heavy. This is why Champagne works with everything from brie to aged cheddar, and why Sauvignon Blanc is such a reliable cheese wine.

When in doubt, go acidic.

## The rule that's mostly wrong

"Red wine with cheese" is repeated constantly and is routinely bad advice. The tannins in full-bodied reds clash with the fat in most cheeses — the tannins feel harsh and the cheese brings out metallic notes. 

The exceptions: aged hard cheeses (Parmigiano-Reggiano, aged Manchego, aged cheddar) can handle bigger reds because they're low in moisture and high in salt and umami, which soften the perception of tannins.

<WineTip type="tip">
  If you want red wine with your cheese board, choose lower-tannin reds. Pinot Noir, Gamay (Beaujolais), Barbera, and Grenache all pair better with cheese than Cabernet Sauvignon or Nebbiolo.
</WineTip>

## The regional shortcut

When in doubt, pair wine and cheese from the same region. Local food and local wine developed together — they tend to work.

- **Loire Valley:** Muscadet or Sancerre with local goat cheeses
- **Burgundy:** Red or white Burgundy with Époisses or Comté
- **Piedmont:** Barolo or Barbaresco with Parmigiano or aged Taleggio
- **Rioja:** Tempranillo with Manchego

<WineTip type="note">
  This works because the wine and the cheese literally grew up together — same soil, same climate, same culinary tradition. The regional shortcut isn't magic, it's just accumulated local knowledge.
</WineTip>

## Building an actual cheese board

For a mixed board with several cheeses:

1. **Anchor it with a high-acid white** — Champagne, Chablis, Sauvignon Blanc, or dry Riesling. This works with everything.
2. **Add a lighter red** if you want — Pinot Noir or Beaujolais handles most cheeses without the tannin problem.
3. **Skip the big reds** unless you're serving exclusively aged hard cheeses.
4. **Consider a sweet wine** — Sauternes with Roquefort is one of the great pairings in all of food and wine.

The goal isn't to find the "perfect" pairing for each individual cheese. It's to find one or two wines that work across the whole board.
