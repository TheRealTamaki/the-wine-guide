Tasting wine properly doesn't require a fancy vocabulary or years of experience. It requires slowing down and paying attention. This guide covers what that actually looks like — in four steps.

## Step 1: Look at it

Hold the glass up to a light source — a white wall works fine. You're checking color, clarity, and viscosity.

Color tells you more than you'd expect. A deep ruby red suggests a full-bodied red. A pale straw yellow usually means a lighter, crisper white. Browning at the edges in a red can mean it's older or oxidized. Pale rims on a red wine often indicate lower alcohol or a cooler climate.

Swirl the glass and watch how the wine moves back down. Those streaks (called "legs" or "tears") are often cited as a sign of quality, but they're really just indicating alcohol and sugar content — thicker legs mean more of both.

## Step 2: Smell it — twice

Smell first without swirling. This gives you the quieter, more delicate aromas that disappear once you agitate the wine.

Then swirl and smell again. The swirling introduces oxygen and releases the more volatile aromatic compounds. This is where you get the full picture.

<WineTip type="tip">
  Don't overthink the aroma description. If it smells like something — black cherry, cut grass, toast, petrol — just say that. You don't need to identify 27 specific compounds to enjoy the experience.
</WineTip>

You're looking for two things: complexity (how many distinct aromas can you find?) and quality (are they pleasant and integrated, or is something off?).

**Red wine aromas to look for:** dark fruit (blackberry, plum, cherry), dried herbs, spice, leather, earth, oak-related notes like vanilla and toast.

**White wine aromas to look for:** citrus, stone fruit (peach, apricot), floral notes, green apple, hay, mineral notes, oak influence if applicable.

## Step 3: Taste it (and pay attention)

Take a small sip and let it sit on your tongue for a few seconds before swallowing.

You're evaluating several things simultaneously:

- **Sweetness** — felt on the tip of the tongue, immediately
- **Acidity** — that mouthwatering, tart quality. High acid wines make your mouth water; low acid wines feel flatter
- **Tannins** — the drying, grippy sensation in your gums and cheeks. Present in reds, mostly absent in whites
- **Body** — how heavy or light the wine feels. Think of it like the difference between skim milk and whole milk
- **Alcohol** — felt as warmth at the back of your throat

<WineTip type="note">
  Acidity and tannins are often confused. Acidity is that salivation-inducing tartness. Tannins are the drying, astringent grip — like strong tea. Both are signs of structure, but they feel completely different.
</WineTip>

## Step 4: The finish

After you swallow, notice how long the flavors linger. This is the "finish" or "length."

A short finish (flavors disappear within a few seconds) often indicates a simpler, lower-quality wine. A long finish (flavors persist for 30 seconds or more) is usually a sign of a well-made wine with depth.

Quality wines often show a finish that evolves — the flavors shift slightly after you've swallowed, revealing something new.

## What to do with all of this

You don't need to score wines or fill out a tasting sheet. The goal is to build a mental library of what you like and why. Over time, paying attention this way means you'll get better at predicting which wines you'll enjoy before you buy them.

Start with two or three wines side by side — same grape, different producers or regions. The contrast makes everything much easier to notice.
