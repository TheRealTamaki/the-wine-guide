Here's the problem with cheap red wine: most of it is forgettable. Smooth enough to drink, bland enough to forget, not worth buying twice. The labels all look the same, the back-label descriptions could apply to literally any red wine ever made, and you won't know if it's any good until you've already paid for it.

But hidden in that $20-and-under shelf are some genuinely excellent bottles. Wines that taste like they wandered in from the $35 section and decided to stay. This list is those bottles.

Every pick here has been tried multiple times. No one-hit wonders, no flukes.

<WineTip type="tip">
  Prices vary by state and retailer. These are average national prices, so yours might be a couple of dollars either way.
</WineTip>

## 1. Cline Ancient Vines Zinfandel, ~$14

These vines are 50 to 100+ years old in Contra Costa County, and you can taste the difference. Old vines produce less fruit but concentrate everything. So instead of the jammy fruit-bomb Zinfandel you might be picturing, this is layered: blackberry jam, black pepper, a whisper of vanilla from the oak.

This is the bottle you bring to a barbecue when you want someone to ask "what is this?" in a good way.

## 2. Bodegas Borsao Tres Picos Garnacha, ~$18

Spanish Garnacha from old vines in Campo de Borja. Sometimes called "the Garnacha capital of the world," which is exactly the kind of thing you can casually drop at a dinner party after you pour this.

Dark cherries, dried violets, a touch of earth, supple tannins, and a finish that goes on longer than it has any right to at this price. It drinks like a $35 wine, and that's not hyperbole.

## 3. Louis Jadot Beaujolais-Villages, ~$14

If you think Beaujolais is just that thin, banana-scented Nouveau stuff that shows up every November, this will change your mind completely.

Jadot's Beaujolais-Villages is made from Gamay in the better village appellations, and it has real depth: bright cherry, raspberry, a little earthiness, and the kind of easy food-friendliness that makes it work with almost anything you put on a plate.

Throw it in the fridge for 30 minutes before opening. Slightly chilled Gamay with roast chicken is one of life's underrated pleasures.

<WineTip type="note">
  Gamay is the grape behind all Beaujolais. It makes lighter, lower-tannin reds that are brilliant slightly chilled. Think of it as Pinot Noir's more easygoing cousin.
</WineTip>

## 4. Crios de Susana Balbo Malbec, ~$16

Susana Balbo is one of Argentina's most respected winemakers, and this is her "accessible" Malbec. It still drinks better than most Malbecs at double the price. Plum, dark chocolate, violets, soft tannins. No harsh edges, no heavy-handed oak. Clean winemaking that lets the grape do the talking.

When someone tells you they "like red wine" and can't be more specific than that? Pour them this. It's the universal crowd-pleaser.

## 5. Chateau Ste. Michelle Columbia Valley Merlot, ~$12

Merlot gets a bad rap, and it's largely undeserved (blame one line in *Sideways* for that). Good Merlot, and this is good Merlot, is soft cherry fruit, a touch of chocolate, smooth tannins, and enough structure to actually age for 3-5 years if you felt like holding onto it.

At $12, this is arguably the best value on the entire list. It's the bottle that's always in the fridge door at my place.
