The $20-and-under category contains a lot of garbage. It also contains some genuinely excellent wine. The problem is that the labels all look the same, the descriptions are useless, and you can't tell what's worth buying until you've already spent the money.

This list cuts through that. Every bottle here has been tested multiple times, holds up to reasonable scrutiny, and delivers quality you'd expect from a $30+ wine.

<WineTip type="tip">
  Price varies by state and retailer. These are average national prices — you may find them slightly cheaper or pricier depending on where you shop.
</WineTip>

## 1. Cline Ancient Vines Zinfandel — ~$14

Old-vine Zinfandel from Contra Costa County. These vines are 50–100+ years old, which produces more concentrated, complex fruit than younger plantings. You get blackberry jam, black pepper, and a hint of vanilla from the oak — rich without being jammy or overblown.

It punches well above its price. This is the bottle you bring to a barbecue when you actually care about what people are drinking.

## 2. Bodegas Borsao Campo de Borja Tres Picos Garnacha — ~$18

Spanish Garnacha at its best for this price. Made from old Grenache vines in Campo de Borja, this has serious depth — dark cherries, dried violets, a touch of earth — with supple tannins and a long finish. It drinks like a $35 wine.

Parker gave it a 90. You don't need to care about Parker scores to appreciate that this is an exceptional bottle for $18.

## 3. Louis Jadot Beaujolais-Villages — ~$14

If you think Beaujolais is just Nouveau (the thin, banana-scented stuff that shows up every November), this will change your mind. Jadot's Beaujolais-Villages is made primarily from Gamay in the better classified village appellations, with genuine depth: bright cherry, raspberry, a little earthiness, and the kind of food-friendliness that makes it work with almost anything.

Serve it slightly chilled (30 minutes in the fridge before opening). It's one of the most versatile food wines at any price.

<WineTip type="note">
  Gamay is the grape of Beaujolais. It makes lighter, lower-tannin reds that are often better served slightly chilled. Think of it as a more elegant, less fruit-bomby Pinot Noir.
</WineTip>

## 4. Crios de Susana Balbo Malbec — ~$16

Argentine Malbec from one of the country's most respected winemakers. This is a benchmark for what good, affordable Malbec looks like: plum, dark chocolate, violets, soft tannins. No harsh edges, no excessive oak, just clean, well-made wine.

If you have people over who say they "like red wine" without being specific, this is what you pour.

## 5. Château Ste. Michelle Columbia Valley Merlot — ~$12

Washington State Merlot from one of the region's foundational producers. Merlot gets unfairly maligned (blame *Sideways*), but good examples like this one are genuinely excellent: soft cherry fruit, a little chocolate, smooth tannins, and enough structure to age 3–5 years if you want to hold it.

At $12, it's one of the best values on this list.
