## Persona framing

**Primary buyer:** Three overlapping clusters, all present in the same search session.

**Cluster A — The Accidental Gifter (largest by volume).** Has no category knowledge. Buying for a parent, relative, or colleague who "likes port." Navigates by brand recognition (Graham's, Taylor's) or staff recommendation. Genuinely unsure whether Tawny and Ruby are quality tiers or different flavour profiles. Anxiety: choosing wrong in front of a gift recipient. Budget: open-ended, often $25–60.

**Cluster B — The New Convert.** Tried Fonseca Bin 27 or Graham's Six Grapes once, loved it, now wants to understand whether Tawny is worth exploring. Treats the category as a rabbit hole they've just found. Budget: $20–40. Will spend more once they understand what they're buying.

**Cluster C — The Occasion Buyer.** Buys port once a year for Christmas, Boxing Day, or a cheese board. Comes back to the same bottle, or tries one new thing per year. Pairs with Stilton and walnuts. Low urgency to understand the category deeply — wants confident guidance and to not overthink it.

**Top objections Lia must address:**

1. **Tawny vs Ruby confusion (P1 — most critical).** Buyers routinely treat these as quality tiers rather than different flavour categories. Tawny = nutty, oxidative, caramel, dried fruit. Ruby = fresh, fruity, jammy, dark chocolate. Lia must address this at article structure level before the wine listings, not as a footnote. Recommended framing: "These are two different wines, not two rungs on a quality ladder. If someone asks you what port tastes like, the honest answer is: it depends which kind." One sentence anchor, then let the style-bucketed H2 structure do the rest.

2. **"Port is too sweet / too heavy" (P3).** Often a reaction to cheap domestic "port-style" product or an entry-level Ruby Reserve drunk warm. Aged Tawnies and quality LBVs have sweetness balanced by oxidative complexity or structured tannins. Frame: "If the last port you tried came out of someone's parents' liquor cabinet, try a Graham's 20 Year Tawny before writing the category off." Anchor on balance, not sweetness level.

3. **Price confusion (P5).** Buyers don't know whether $15 is fine or whether they need $50+ to get something good. Anchor tiers early: $15–25 = solid Ruby Reserve entry point. $30–45 = 10yr Tawny or quality LBV. $50–65 = 20yr Tawny (gift and celebration tier). Don't bury this in wine descriptions. WineTip #1 pricing disclaimer is the right vehicle.

**Purchase triggers:** Christmas / Boxing Day gifting; birthday-vintage gifting ("a bottle as old as he is"); cheese board moment; first conversion after a Ruby Reserve.

**Tone guidance for Lia:** Conversational, opinionated, and warm — not a textbook. Buyers are slightly embarrassed they don't know the difference between Tawny and Ruby; the article should make them feel smart for reading it, not like they should have known already. Use the language bank: "very easy to drink and a bit dangerous in that sense," "it's a slippery but fun slope," "best bang for the buck when buying retail." Avoid tasting-note lectures. Voice guide: no em-dashes, no juxtaposition patterns ("not X, just Y"), second person always, contractions always.
