### H2: What Is a Wine Blend, Exactly?
- Definition: multiple grape varieties combined to make a single wine
- Contrast with single-varietal wines
- Three types: Old World blends named by region, New World blends named by variety or creative name, "single varietal blends" (75% rule)
- The 75% rule (US TTB), 85% EU baseline, Oregon's 90%

### H2: Why Do Winemakers Blend Wine?
- Balance: one grape's weakness fixed by another's strength (Merlot softening Cab Sauv's grip)
- Complexity: layers of aroma and texture from different varieties
- Consistency: mitigating vintage variation by blending across vineyards or parcels
- Regional tradition: what grows together goes together (climate as a guide)
- Brief mention of economic factors (not the lead — not reader-first)

### H2: What Are the Most Famous Wine Blends in the World?
Sub-sections:
- **Bordeaux (red):** Cab Sauv + Merlot + Cab Franc + Petit Verdot + Malbec. Left bank vs right bank split.
- **Bordeaux (white):** Sauvignon Blanc + Sémillon
- **GSM / Rhône:** Grenache + Syrah + Mourvèdre. Châteauneuf-du-Pape allows 13+ varieties.
- **Champagne:** Chardonnay + Pinot Noir + Pinot Meunier. Non-vintage blends across years.
- **Chianti / Super Tuscans:** Sangiovese-based. Super Tuscans broke the rules by adding Cab Sauv/Merlot.
- **Rioja:** Tempranillo + Garnacha + Graciano
- **Australian GSM:** Grenache + Syrah + Mourvèdre transplanted to Barossa, McLaren Vale
- **Meritage (American Bordeaux-style):** Napa, Washington, etc.
- **Field blends:** oldest form — grapes planted together, harvested together

### H2: How Do You Read a Wine Blend Label?
- Old World: label says region, not grapes (Châteauneuf-du-Pape tells you nothing about the mix)
- New World: if it says a variety name, 75% rule applies in US
- If labeled "Red Blend" or "GSM" or "Meritage" — what each tells you
- Back label often has more info; winery websites have tech sheets

### H2: When Do Wine Blends Shine Compared to Single-Varietal Wines?
- Blends for balance and food friendliness
- Single varietals for purity of expression (Burgundy Pinot, Alsace Riesling)
- Neither is superior — they're different tools
- Practical guide: if you want complexity with dinner, blends often win
