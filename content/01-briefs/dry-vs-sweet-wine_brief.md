## Article Architecture

### Intro block (mandatory order)
1. `<QuickAnswer>` — direct answer to what makes a wine dry vs sweet
2. Hook paragraph — scenario: you're at the bottle shop, someone says "I only drink dry" but keeps picking fruit-forward Pinot Gris
3. Fascination bullets (4-6)

### H2 sections (all phrased as questions)
1. **What Makes a Wine Dry or Sweet?** — mechanism: yeast + fermentation + residual sugar; fermentation stopped early = sweet; run to completion = dry
2. **Why Do Some Dry Wines Taste Sweet?** — fruit-forward aromatics (Pinot Gris, New Zealand Sauv Blanc), oak influence, glycerol; key insight: fruity aroma ≠ residual sugar
3. **How Do You Read a Label to Know?** — term glossary: brut, extra dry, sec, abboccato, dolce; alcohol clue (high ABV = often drier); region signals (German Riesling: Kabinett/Spätlese/Auslese scale)
4. **Dry vs Sweet: Where They Differ on the Palate** — sensory: tannins, acidity, mouthfeel; sweet wines feel rounder, lower acid at front; dry wines feel tighter, more grip
5. **When Should I Choose a Dry Wine?** — savoury food pairings; red meat, pasta, cheese; aperitif drinking; when you want the food to shine
6. **When Should I Choose a Sweet Wine?** — spicy food (Thai, Indian); dessert bridge; foie gras; strong blue cheese
7. **Sweetness Scale: From Bone Dry to Dessert** — comparison table
8. **Frequently Asked Questions** (4-6 Qs)
9. Closing CTA (ComparePicksCard to types-of-wine or related)

## Voice Notes
- Tone: sharp, funny friend who knows wine. Second person throughout.
- The "I only drink dry wine" persona is gold: most people who say this actually prefer fruit-forward styles. Lean into that without being condescending.
- Fruity ≠ sweet point: explain it naturally, don't lecture. One clear analogy is enough.
- No em-dashes. No "not X, just Y." No banned phrases.
- H2s phrased as real questions a person would type.
