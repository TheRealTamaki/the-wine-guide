## What Are Red Wines?

Red wine is made from dark-skinned grapes. The skins stay in contact with the juice during fermentation, which is what gives red wine its colour, its tannins, and a lot of its flavour.

Tannins are the grippy, drying sensation you feel on your gums after a sip of a big red. They're not a flaw. They're part of what makes certain reds food-friendly: the structure softens against protein and fat, which is why a Cabernet Sauvignon tastes so good next to a steak.

The body of a red wine sits on a spectrum from light to full. A light-bodied Pinot Noir from Burgundy or Central Otago tastes delicate, with cherry and earthy notes and gentle tannins. A full-bodied Cabernet Sauvignon from Napa or Coonawarra is rich, dense, and built to age.

Key red wine grapes to know:

- **Cabernet Sauvignon:** Full-bodied, firm tannins, blackberry and black pepper notes with a hint of tobacco. Originating in Bordeaux but now widely planted from Napa Valley to Coonawarra in Australia.
- **Merlot:** Softer and rounder than Cabernet, with plum, chocolate, and dark cherry fruit flavors. Often used in blends to add body and smoothness.
- **Pinot Noir:** Light body, high acidity, silky texture. Think raspberry, cherry, and earthy undertones. The most finicky grape to grow, and the most rewarding when it's right.
- **Syrah / Shiraz:** The same grape, two personalities. Syrah from the Rhône Valley in France is peppery and herbal. Shiraz in Australia is bold, dark-fruited, and sometimes smoky.
- **Malbec:** Originally from France, now the signature red of Argentina. Juicy, with intense blackberry and plum fruit, smooth tannins.
- **Grenache (also Garnacha in Spanish):** Red-fruit driven with raspberry and a touch of spice. Medium body, often used in southern French and Spanish blends.
- **Tempranillo:** Spain's most important red grape. Dried fruit, leather, and toasted oak notes. The backbone of Rioja.

## What Are White Wines?

White wine is made from white (or yellow-green) grapes, and the skins are removed before fermentation. That's why it's pale. Without skin contact, there are no tannins, which gives white wine its lighter, crisper character.

The defining quality of most white wines is acidity. High acidity is what makes white wine taste refreshing, food-friendly, and alive in the glass. A good white with grilled fish or a creamy pasta does something a red can't.

White wines range from bone dry (a Chablis or unoaked Sauvignon Blanc) to quite sweet (a German Riesling). Style (oaked vs unoaked) makes a big difference too. A Chardonnay with no oak tastes lean and citrus-driven. The same grape barrel-aged tastes creamy and rich, with notes of vanilla and butter.

Key white wine grapes to know:

- **Chardonnay:** The world's most widely planted white grape. Versatile enough to range from crisp and mineral (Chablis) to buttery and full (Napa Valley oaked Chardonnay). Common flavor profile: apple, citrus, and sometimes tropical fruit in warmer climates.
- **Sauvignon Blanc:** High acidity, zesty, herbaceous. Think citrus, lime, and passion fruit. New Zealand (Marlborough) made this grape famous worldwide, but it's equally at home in the Loire Valley in France.
- **Pinot Gris / Pinot Grigio:** Same grape, different styles. Pinot Grigio from Italy is light, crisp, and neutral with apple and pear notes. Pinot Gris from Alsace is richer, with more texture, melon, and sometimes a mild sweetness.
- **Riesling:** One of the most misunderstood white grapes. Aromatic white with green apple, lime, and floral notes. Can be bone dry and highly acidic or lusciously sweet (late harvest). Always food-friendly.

## What Is Rosé Wine?

Rosé is made from red wine grapes, but the skins are only left in contact with the juice for a short time. The result is a wine that picks up some colour and a hint of tannin, but sits between red and white in style.

Most dry rosé is crisp, light, and fruit-forward: strawberry, watermelon, peach. It's the wine that works everywhere because it has the freshness of a white and just enough grip to stand up to food.

Sweet rosé is a different story. White Zinfandel from California is the most well-known example: pink, low in alcohol, noticeably sweet. It has its audience. Dry Provençal rosé is what you're ordering when you want something elegant.

The key thing to check: dry vs sweet. The label won't always say it outright, but the region often tells you. Provence rosé is almost always dry. White Zinfandel is almost always sweet.

## What Is Sparkling Wine?

Sparkling wine is any wine with carbonation. The bubbles come from a second fermentation that traps carbon dioxide in the bottle. What you call it depends on where it's made.

Champagne comes from the Champagne region of France. It's made with Chardonnay, Pinot Noir, and sometimes Meunier, using a method that's strictly controlled. The taste is toasty, biscuity, and refined, with high acidity.

Prosecco comes from northeast Italy. It's lighter, fruitier, and made in tanks rather than individual bottles. That's why it's cheaper and tastes fresher, with less complexity.

Cava is the Spanish equivalent. Made using the same method as Champagne but with different grapes (including Macabeo and Parellada), it delivers a lot of quality at a lower price point.

Outside those three, sparkling wine is the catch-all. Australian sparkling Shiraz is a style all of its own. Crémant is French sparkling wine made in regions outside Champagne, and it's consistently underrated.

## What Are Dessert Wines?

Dessert wines are sweet wines. They get their sweetness in different ways: from leaving grapes on the vine until they're very ripe, from drying grapes to concentrate the sugar, or from stopping fermentation before all the sugar converts to alcohol.

Moscato is the most approachable: light, low in alcohol, gently fizzy in its Moscato d'Asti form, with peach and floral notes. If you're new to sweet wines, this is where to start.

Late-harvest wines (like German Spätlese or Auslese Rieslings) concentrate the sugar by leaving grapes on the vine late into the season. Sauternes from Bordeaux takes it further. The grapes are affected by a noble rot called Botrytis cinerea, which shrivels them and intensifies the sweetness to something remarkable.

Ice wine is another category: grapes are left to freeze on the vine, then pressed while frozen to extract an intensely sweet juice.

The balance that makes a good dessert wine is the ratio of sweetness to acidity. Sugar without acidity tastes flat and cloying. When the two are in balance, the wine feels rich but fresh.

## What Are Fortified Wines?

Fortified wine has had a grape spirit (usually brandy) added to it, which raises the alcohol level and stops fermentation. The result is a wine with more alcohol than usual (typically 17-22% ABV) and often a noticeable sweetness.

Port is the most famous. It comes from the Douro Valley in Portugal and comes in several styles: Ruby Port is dark, sweet, and fruit-forward. Tawny Port has been aged in small barrels, turning amber in colour and picking up nutty, dried-fruit flavours. Vintage Port is the serious collector's style, made only in exceptional years.

Sherry is dry fortified wine from Andalusia in Spain. The styles range from Fino (pale, dry, nutty) to Amontillado (amber, more complex) to Pedro Ximénez (intensely sweet, like liquid raisins). Dry Sherry with a plate of jamón ibérico is one of the great food pairings.

Madeira comes from a Portuguese island and is nearly indestructible. The wine goes through a deliberate heating process that makes it last for decades once opened.

## What Is Orange Wine?

Orange wine has nothing to do with oranges. It's a white wine made with extended skin contact, meaning the grape skins stay in the juice during fermentation, the same way red wine is made.

The skin contact gives orange wine its amber-to-deep-orange colour. It also adds tannins, which is unusual for a white wine. The result is something that sits between white wine and red wine in structure: earthy, textured, sometimes funky, with a grippy finish you wouldn't expect from something made with white grapes.

Popular grapes for orange wine include Pinot Gris, Gewurztraminer, and Georgian varieties like Rkatsiteli. Georgia (the country) has been making skin-contact wines in clay vessels called qvevri for thousands of years.

If you're coming to orange wine for the first time, expect it to taste different from anything else in the glass. The earthiness and tannin can catch people off guard. Start with a lighter style.

## What Is Natural Wine?

Natural wine is a loose term for wine made with minimal intervention. There's no legal definition, which is where the debates start.

In general, natural wine means: organically or biodynamically grown grapes, wild yeast fermentation (no added commercial yeast), little or no added sulphites, and no additives used to manipulate colour, flavour, or texture. The idea is that the wine reflects the vineyard and the vintage as directly as possible.

Some natural wines are extraordinary: vibrant, alive, and unlike anything you'd get from a conventional winery. Some are cloudy, funky, or unstable in ways that divide opinion sharply. The category rewards curiosity, but it also means doing your research before spending money on a bottle.

Natural wine often overlaps with organic and biodynamic wine. Organic wine means the grapes were grown without synthetic pesticides. Biodynamic takes it further, treating the vineyard as a self-sustaining ecosystem. Both can be made with or without the additional winemaking constraints that define natural wine.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the difference between red and white wine?

Red wine is made with dark-skinned grapes and includes the skins in fermentation, which gives it colour, tannins, and fuller body. White wine uses light-skinned grapes and removes the skins before fermentation, so it stays pale and has no tannins. Both styles span the full range from dry to sweet, but the tannin difference is what you'll find most obvious in the glass. It's also the clearest reason why a dry red pairs well with red meat and white wine suits lighter food.

### Is rosé a red or white wine?

Rosé sits in its own category. It's made from red wine grapes but the skins only stay in contact with the juice briefly, giving it its pink colour and a lighter style than a full red. The taste profile is closer to white wine for most dry rosés: fresh, fruity, high in acidity. Sweet rosés like White Zinfandel read differently but are made the same way.

### What is the sweetest type of wine?

Dessert wines are the sweetest category. Ice wine and Sauternes are among the most intensely sweet styles. Within more everyday wines, Moscato d'Asti is a popular sweet option with low alcohol and a light fizz. Sweet styles of Riesling, Gewurztraminer, and Vouvray (Chenin Blanc) are also worth knowing.

### What does "dry" mean in wine?

Dry means the wine has very little residual sugar. During fermentation, yeast converts grape sugar into alcohol. A dry wine has gone through complete fermentation, so almost no sugar remains. It doesn't mean the wine lacks flavour or fruit. A dry Riesling with high acidity can taste fruity and vibrant while being technically dry.

### What is the difference between sparkling wine and Champagne?

Champagne is sparkling wine made specifically in the Champagne region of France from a defined set of grapes using a specific method. All Champagne is sparkling wine, but not all sparkling wine is Champagne. Prosecco (Italy), Cava (Spain), Crémant (France, outside Champagne), and Australian sparkling wines are all sparkling wine made outside that region or method.

### What type of wine should a beginner start with?

Start with something approachable in the style you already like. If you prefer lighter flavours, a dry Pinot Grigio or a Sauvignon Blanc are good choices. If you want something a bit richer, an unoaked Chardonnay is similarly easy to enjoy. For red, a Pinot Noir is the gentlest starting point because the tannins are soft and the fruit flavors are fresh rather than intense. Moscato is the entry point for sweet wines. Learn what you like through a few well-chosen bottles, then branch out from there.
