§ 03   A beginner's field guide

Merlot

/mɛrˈloʊ/  ·  "mair-loh"

A smooth, full-bodied red wine from a blue-black Bordeaux grape. Softer than Cabernet Sauvignon, rounder than Pinot Noir — with plush dark fruit, velvety tannins, and an effortless drinkability that made it one of the world's most planted grapes.

The grape itself

Blue-black and thin-skinned, Bordeaux's workhorse.

Merlot berries are blue-black with thin skins — the thinner skin means fewer tannins than Cabernet Sauvignon, giving the wine its signature soft, velvety texture. The name comes from merle, the French word for blackbird, likely a nod to the grape's deep color.

  1. Growing

    An early ripener sensitive to spring frost. Thrives in clay soils that retain moisture. Warm days build plummy richness; cool nights hold freshness.

  2. Harvest

    Picked in early-to-mid autumn — timing is critical. Too early = green, thin wine. Too late = jammy and flat. The window is narrow.

  3. Crushing & destemming

    Grapes are crushed and stems removed. The juice (must) stays in contact with the skins — this is where red wine's color and tannin come from.

  4. Maceration

    Skins steep in the fermenting juice for 7–14 days. Gentle extraction is key for Merlot — too long and you lose its signature softness.

  5. Fermentation

    Yeast converts sugar to alcohol at 22–28°C. Warmer than whites, which helps extract color and flavor from the skins.

  6. Oak aging

    12–18 months in French oak barrels softens tannins and adds cedar, vanilla, and spice. Premium bottles age longer — top Pomerol can need a decade.
