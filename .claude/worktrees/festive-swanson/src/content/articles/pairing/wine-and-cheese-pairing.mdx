Wine and cheese is one of those pairings everyone *says* they know about, and most of the advice out there is either uselessly vague ("pair red with aged cheese") or so prescriptive it's paralysing ("only Sancerre with chevre, and only on a Tuesday when Mercury is in retrograde").

Neither helps you at an actual cheese board with actual guests arriving in 20 minutes.

Here's what you actually need to know.

## The one rule that never lets you down

**High-acid wines pair with nearly any cheese.** That's it. That's the cheat code.

Acidity cuts through fat and salt, refreshes your palate between bites, and keeps everything from feeling heavy. This is why Champagne works with everything from brie to aged cheddar, why Sauvignon Blanc is the Swiss Army knife of cheese wines, and why a bone-dry Riesling handles things most reds can't.

When in doubt, reach for something acidic. You'll rarely go wrong.

## The rule everyone repeats that's mostly wrong

"Red wine goes with cheese." You've heard it a hundred times. It's routinely terrible advice.

Here's why: the tannins in full-bodied reds clash with the fat in most cheeses. The tannins taste harsh, the cheese brings out metallic notes, and both end up tasting worse than they would alone. Pour yourself a glass of Cabernet and bite into a piece of brie. You'll see exactly what I mean.

**The exceptions:** aged, hard cheeses can handle bigger reds. Parmigiano-Reggiano, aged Manchego, aged cheddar. They're low in moisture, high in salt and umami, and those qualities soften the tannins. So if you're dead set on red wine at your cheese night, serve it with the hard stuff.

<WineTip type="tip">
  Want red wine with your cheese board but don't want the tannin clash? Go for lighter reds: Pinot Noir, Gamay (Beaujolais), Barbera, or Grenache. They've got enough red-wine character to satisfy without the mouth-drying tannins that fight the cheese.
</WineTip>

## The lazy shortcut that always works

Pair wine and cheese from the same region. They grew up together, same soil, same climate, same food culture, so they tend to get along.

- **Loire Valley:** Muscadet or Sancerre with local goat cheeses
- **Burgundy:** Red or white Burgundy with Epoisses or Comte
- **Piedmont:** Barolo or Barbaresco with Parmigiano or aged Taleggio
- **Rioja:** Tempranillo with Manchego

<WineTip type="note">
  This isn't some romantic theory. Generations of people ate and drank what was local, and the pairings that survived are the ones that worked. It's accumulated wisdom baked into the food culture.
</WineTip>

## How to build a cheese board that doesn't stress you out

Trying to find the "perfect" wine for each individual cheese is a trap. You'll drive yourself mad and end up opening six bottles for four guests. Instead:

1. **Anchor with a high-acid white.** Champagne, Chablis, Sauvignon Blanc, or dry Riesling. This handles everything on the board.
2. **Add a lighter red if you want.** Pinot Noir or Beaujolais plays well with most cheeses without the tannin problem.
3. **Skip the big reds** unless your entire board is aged hard cheeses (in which case, go for it. Barolo and a wedge of Parmigiano is spectacular).
4. **Throw in a sweet wine if you're feeling fancy.** Sauternes with Roquefort is one of those pairings that makes you close your eyes and rethink your life choices. In the best way.

That's it. Two wines, maybe three, and they work across the whole board. Your guests will think you're a genius. You can keep that secret.
