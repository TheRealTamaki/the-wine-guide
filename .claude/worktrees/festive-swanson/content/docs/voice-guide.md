# Brand Voice Guide — The Wine Guide

Last updated: 2026-04-14

**MANDATORY: Read this entire file before writing any content for the site.**

---

> **HARD RULE: NO EM-DASHES. EVER.**
> Do not use `—` anywhere in any content, in any context, for any reason.
> Use a full stop, a comma, or a colon instead. This rule has no exceptions.

---

## Core Voice

Talk like a sharp, funny friend who knows wine — not a sommelier performing expertise, not a textbook reciting facts. You're the person at the dinner party who says "try this one, trust me" and everyone does, because you've never steered them wrong.

The reader should feel like they just got smarter without realising they were being taught.

---

## The 10 Principles

These are the non-negotiable rules that govern everything we write — homepage copy, article intros, product descriptions, CTAs, email subject lines, all of it.

### 1. Benefits over process

Never lead with what wine IS. Lead with what it DOES for the reader.

- **No:** "This guide covers the major red grape varieties and their characteristics."
- **Yes:** "Find your new favourite red — the one you'll keep coming back to on a Tuesday night."

The reader doesn't want a wine education. They want to feel confident picking a bottle, impress someone at dinner, or stop wasting money on wine they don't enjoy. Speak to THAT.

### 2. Talk to the right person

Our reader is a curious everyday drinker — not a collector, not a total beginner. They buy wine at bottle shops and restaurants. They know what they like but want to like more things. They don't want to be talked down to, and they don't want a lecture.

Write copy that makes THIS person think "yes, that's me." If the copy could apply to literally anyone, it's too vague.

- **No:** "Wine is a wonderful beverage enjoyed by millions."
- **Yes:** "You know that moment in the bottle shop where you just... grab the same Pinot Noir again? Let's fix that."

### 3. Get in their head

What does our reader think about at 10pm when they're scrolling their phone with a glass in hand? What do they wish they knew? What are they slightly embarrassed about not knowing?

Things like:
- "I always pick the second-cheapest wine on the menu and I have no idea if that's dumb."
- "Someone said this wine was 'oaky' and I nodded but I have no clue what that means."
- "I want to bring a good bottle to dinner but I don't want to spend $60 to be safe."

THOSE worries, wishes, and quiet frustrations should drive the copy. Not what WE think sounds important.

### 4. Specific beats vague — always

Concrete details make everything more compelling. They paint pictures. They build trust.

- **No:** "A great wine for pairing with food."
- **Yes:** "The kind of wine that makes a bowl of spaghetti bolognese feel like a night out."

- **No:** "An affordable option."
- **Yes:** "Under $18, and it drinks like something twice the price."

Name the grape. Name the region. Name the dish. Name the price point. Name the moment. Details are what separate copy that gets a nod from copy that gets a click.

### 5. Conversational, not clinical

Read everything out loud. If you sound like a ridiculous robot, rewrite it. Use contractions. Write the way people actually talk.

- **No:** "It is important to allow the wine to breathe for approximately 30 minutes prior to consumption."
- **Yes:** "Pop it open 30 minutes before you pour. It'll taste noticeably better."

Wine already intimidates people. Our job is to make it feel easy, not academic.

### 6. Show it, don't say it

Never TELL the reader something is exciting, delicious, fun, or interesting. Make them FEEL it through the writing itself.

- **No:** "This is a really exciting wine region."
- **Yes:** "Ten years ago, nobody was talking about Etna wines. Now sommeliers won't shut up about them — and once you try one, you'll get why."

Adjectives like "amazing," "incredible," "fantastic" are empty calories. Cut them. Replace them with something the reader can actually picture.

### 7. Headlines that earn the scroll

Every headline, subheading, and bold line is prime real estate. Don't waste it on throat-clearing.

- **No:** "Introduction to Red Wine" / "About Our Guide" / "Welcome"
- **Yes:** "Stop Overpaying for Bad Pinot Noir" / "The $15 Bottle That Punches Way Above Its Weight"

A good headline either promises a specific benefit, provokes curiosity, or calls out to the exact reader we want. Ideally all three.

### 8. Reader-centred, not "all about us"

The copy is about YOU (the reader). Not about us, our expertise, our passion, our story. Everything we write should make the reader feel seen and served.

- **No:** "We've spent years curating the finest wine recommendations."
- **Yes:** "You're about to find bottles you'll actually want to reorder — not just try once and forget."

When we do talk about ourselves, it should immediately connect back to what the reader gets out of it.

### 9. Sell the result, not the process

Nobody wants to "learn about tannins." They want to know why that red wine made their mouth feel weird and whether that's a good thing. Every piece of wine knowledge we share should connect to a real-world payoff.

- **No:** "This article will teach you the fundamentals of wine tasting."
- **Yes:** "After this, you'll actually know what you're tasting — and you'll sound like it, too."

Process is spinach. Results are cake. Serve cake.

### 10. Every page gets a next step

Never leave the reader hanging. Every article, every page, every section should make it obvious what to do next. Not with a desperate "CLICK HERE" — with a natural, benefit-driven nudge.

- **No:** "Click here for more articles."
- **Yes:** "Ready to put this to the test? Here are 5 bottles under $20 that'll prove everything you just read."

## Sentence-Level Rules

| Rule | Example |
|------|---------|
| Second person (you/your) — always | "You'll notice..." not "One might notice..." |
| Contractions — always | "it's," "you'll," "don't," "won't" |
| Paragraphs under 4 sentences | Break up walls of text |
| Specific examples over generic | "a 2022 Cotes du Rhone" not "a nice red" |
| Explain jargon naturally in context | "tannins — that dry, grippy feeling" |
| Be opinionated in recommendations | "this is worth trying" not "this may appeal to some" |
| Vary sentence openings | Don't start 3 sentences in a row the same way |
| One idea per paragraph | If you're making two points, split them |

## Banned Punctuation

**No em-dashes.** Not a single one. No `—` anywhere in any content. Use a full stop to break the thought into two sentences instead. If you absolutely need a pause that's softer than a full stop, use a comma or a colon. Em-dashes are a crutch that makes writing feel like it's trying too hard.

## Banned Sentence Patterns

**No juxtaposition setups.** These are sentences that follow the pattern "not X, just Y" or "this isn't X. It's Y." They sound clever the first time. By the third time on the page, they're exhausting. They also signal AI-written copy from a mile away.

Examples of what NOT to write:
- "This isn't a textbook. It's a cheat sheet."
- "Not a lecture, just straight talk."
- "We're not sommeliers. We're regular people who drink wine."
- "It's not about the price tag. It's about what's in the glass."
- "No jargon, no snobbery, just useful advice."
- Any variation of "not X. Just Y." / "no X, just Y" / "less X, more Y"

Instead, just say the positive thing directly. "Here's a cheat sheet." "Straight talk about wine." Say what it IS without needing to contrast it against what it isn't.

## Banned Phrases

These are dead on arrival. Never use them:

- "Have you ever wondered..."
- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "The world of wine"
- "The art of..."
- "Wine is a journey"
- "Let's dive in" / "Let's explore"
- "Without further ado"
- "It goes without saying"
- "At the end of the day"
- "First and foremost"
- "In conclusion"
- "As we all know"
- Any sentence that starts with "Welcome to..."

## Affiliate Mentions

- Mention products only when genuinely relevant — never shoehorn
- Frame as "what I'd grab" or "the one I keep going back to" — not "click here to buy"
- Maximum 2-3 affiliate mentions per article
- The recommendation should feel like advice from a friend, not a sales pitch

## The Gut Check

Before publishing anything, ask:

1. Would I actually say this out loud to a friend?
2. Does the first line make someone want to read the second line?
3. Am I talking about what the reader WANTS, or what I think sounds impressive?
4. Are there any vague adjectives I can replace with specifics?
5. Is there a clear next step?

If any answer is no, rewrite.
