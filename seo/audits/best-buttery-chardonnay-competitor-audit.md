## Gap analysis — what top pages have that we don't

1. **Price-banded "quick picks" box above the fold.** YourWineStore's quick-picks bar (Best Overall / Best Value / Best Budget / Best Unoaked / Best Splurge) is the clearest shopper-intent signpost on the SERP. We have TopPicksInline (3 picks) but don't frame them by purchase use-case.
2. **Explicit price-tier H2 structure.** YourWineStore organises its list as "Best Under $20 / Under $30 / Under $50 / Under $75 / Under $150". Our 8 wines span roughly $15–$70 but are ordered by editorial ranking, not price. Readers with budget constraints have to mentally re-sort.
3. **Oaked vs unoaked explainer.** Paige, Jancis, and YourWineStore FAQ all include this. We cover it inside FAQ ("How do I pick a buttery Chardonnay?") but not as its own standalone block. Lower priority but missing.
4. **Global / non-California picks for the "richer Chardonnay" intent.** Jancis Robinson goes deep into Argentina, Australia, NZ, South Africa, and Burgundy as alternative sources. Our page is 100% California. This is defensible (buttery = California is the mainstream association), but a single "looking further afield?" sidebar mentioning 2–3 non-California richer styles would expand relevance without breaking the California focus.
5. **Wine Enthusiast's "team buttery / team lean" editorial framing.** Not a feature per se, but their article opens with a critic debate ("some love it, some hate it") and invites the reader in. Our intro is voice-led but doesn't do the opposing-view hook.
6. **Cheese pairing guidance.** YourWineStore's FAQ has a full cheese-pairing sub-section. We don't. Low effort to add.

**What we have that they don't (for preservation):**
- WineQuiz routing readers to one of our 8 wines by food + occasion + style preference. No competitor on the SERP has an interactive quiz.
- Malolactic fermentation explainer in the FAQ with diacetyl-level specificity. More detailed than any competitor's "what makes it buttery" explainer.
- Serving temperature WineTip with specific degree range (50–55°F). Wine Enthusiast mentions it; nobody else gives a specific target.

## Novel opportunity (at least one feature no competitor has)

**Add a "Butter Intensity Matrix" above the wine listings — a 2D positioning grid** plotting each of our 8 (or 10) wines on two axes:

- **X-axis: Butter intensity** (1 = subtle / restrained → 5 = full buttery coating)
- **Y-axis: Body / weight** (1 = medium, food-friendly → 5 = rich and layered)

Implemented as a static HTML 2-column list with dot-scale visual (using text characters `●●○○○` / `●●●●●` or a small SVG if an Astro component gets added later). Each of the 8 wines gets a row showing:

| Wine | Butter intensity (●●●●○) | Body (●●●○○) | Best for |
|---|---|---|---|
| Rombauer | ●●●●● | ●●●●● | Ultra-buttery lovers |
| Kendall-Jackson | ●●●○○ | ●●○○○ | Weeknight, budget |
| La Crema | ●●○○○ | ●●●○○ | Food-first, cuts through richness |
| etc. | — | — | — |

No competitor on the SERP plots wines on a 2-dimensional scale. Wine Enthusiast scores for quality (points), YourWineStore segments by price, Jancis segments by region. Segmentation by **taste profile** (how buttery vs how heavy) is the actual purchase question for this keyword, and nobody does it.

This is a genuine structural moat: a Rombauer shopper and a Jordan shopper both want "buttery Chardonnay" but they want *different* wines, and the matrix makes the difference legible in 5 seconds.

**Second novel opportunity (secondary):** "Rombauer alternative by price tier" — a mini decision tree that reads "Rombauer sold out / too expensive? Try Frank Family (same Carneros, ~20% cheaper), La Crema (fresher, acid-balanced), or Kendall-Jackson (under $15)." This substitution framing is what shoppers actually Google when they've heard of Rombauer but can't find it. No competitor has it.
