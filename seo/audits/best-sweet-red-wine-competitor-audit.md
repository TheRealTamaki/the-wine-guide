## Gap analysis — what top pages have that we don't

1. **Per-wine Region / ABV / Tasting Notes data block (Liquor.com):** Every wine gets a consistent 3-attribute quick-reference block before the prose review. We have pairings and critic scores but no inline ABV or explicit region label (region is buried in the prose). ABV matters hugely for sweet reds (7% Brachetto vs 15% Port vs 20% Vintage Port is the whole buying decision).
2. **Category award badges per bottle (Liquor.com, SomeGoodCleanFun pattern):** "Best Overall / Best Under $20 / Best Organic / Best Italian / Best German / Best Greek / Best South African / Best California / Best Semi-Sweet / Best Splurge" — Liquor.com uses 10 unique award categories for 10 bottles. We use numeric 1–8. Awards are scannable and shareable.
3. **Higher primary bottle count if we want to compete with Wine Curmudgeon's breadth:** 26 is excessive but they rank on comprehensiveness. Our 8 primary feels thin next to a SERP where the anchor competitor ships 10 and the comprehensive tier ships 26.
4. **"What makes a wine sweet" educational primer (San Antonio Specialty):** Residual sugar explainer as an opening H2 before the wine list. We have a solid FAQ answer on this but no in-body primer H2.
5. **Sweetness-level indicator per wine (our WineQuiz has this, but the body listings don't):** Our quiz tags wines as "light-sweet / medium-sweet / rich-sweet" but the main body doesn't surface this. A reader scanning the H2s has no way to know Mionetto is lightly sparkling and semi-dry while Allegrini Recioto is thick and dessert-sweet without reading the paragraphs.
6. **Retailer comparison (Liquor.com):** Wine.com / Vivino / Total Wine / Minibar links per bottle. We're monogamous to Wine.com per strategy. Note but not a gap.
7. **Residual sugar grams/litre (none of our competitors have this explicitly):** Curiously missing across the SERP. Quick win for us.

**What nobody has that we already do:** WineQuiz (sweetness-matching), MethodologyBox, 10-question FAQ (FAQ depth here is already above Liquor.com's 3), intro that normalises the preference.

## Novel opportunity (no competitor has this)

**Add a "Sweetness Scale" visual matrix above the wine listings** — a horizontal sweetness spectrum chart with all 8 bottles plotted by two dimensions:

- X-axis: Residual sugar (light-sweet < 30g/L → medium-sweet 30–80g/L → rich-sweet 80–150g/L → dessert-level 150g/L+)
- Y-axis: Body (light & fizzy → medium & still → full & fortified)

Implementation options:
- **Simpler:** A 4-column HTML table: Wine / Sweetness level (1–5 dots) / Body (1–5 dots) / ABV.
- **Richer:** A CSS grid "spectrum chart" with 8 bottle markers positioned by sweetness and body coordinates — similar to a flavour-profile scatter plot.

No competitor on the SERP has a visual sweetness-vs-body scatter, and sweetness is literally the defining variable of this category. This would be the single most useful buying aid on the page.

**Bonus novelty:** An "occasion matrix" H3 tying each wine to occasions (weeknight / dinner party / dessert pairing / gift / aperitivo / post-dinner).
