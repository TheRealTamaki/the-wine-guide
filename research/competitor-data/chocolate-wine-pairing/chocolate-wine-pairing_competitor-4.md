# Pairing Chocolates & Wines: From Mint To Nuts, There's A Perfect Wine For Every Chocolate

## Introduction

When matching wine with chocolate, the foundational principle mirrors food pairing: lighter-flavored chocolates work with lighter-bodied wines, while intense chocolates pair with full-bodied options. However, intensity isn't determined by chocolate type alone. Dark chocolates with delicate ganaches, for example, can be lighter in flavor than white or milk chocolate filled with intense caramel or with spicy or nut centers.

You have two pairing strategies: seek wines sharing the chocolate's flavor notes (nutty, cherry, mint), or explore contrasts. As with all pairings, only you are the arbiter of what works best: There are no absolutes.

### Expert Perspectives

Fortified wines are traditionally recommended due to their sweetness and structure, though experts note this perspective is oversimplified. John Anderson, formerly a vintner, cautions against serving Champagne with chocolate. The dryness and astringency clash. However, demi-sec or sweet Champagne pairs acceptably with white chocolate. Meanwhile, Joan Coukos pairs rose Champagne with raspberry jam-filled chocolate, demonstrating creative possibilities.

Banyuls receives special recognition as considered by many experts to be the perfect wine match for chocolate, with top producers including Domaine de la Rectorie, Domaine du Mas Blanc, and Domaine la Tour Vieille.

## Chocolate & Wine Pairing Chart

### Bittersweet Chocolate (70%-100% cacao)

The most intense variety features bitter, roasted, fruity, earthy, woodsy, ashy, and nutty notes.

Recommended Pairings:
- Banyuls
- Beaujolais
- Bordeaux
- Cabernet Sauvignon
- Cognac/Armagnac
- Grenache
- Malbec
- Marsala
- Merlot
- Mas Amiel Classique
- Orange Muscat
- Tawny Port
- Ruby Port
- Shiraz
- Zinfandel

Key Considerations: The intensity demands stronger red wines with concentrated fruit notes. Despite appearing too tannic, the cocoa butter decreases the astringency and dryness of the tannins and the higher cacao content enables bittersweet chocolate to pair well.

Additional recommendations include Vin Jaune from Jura for plain, high-percentage cacao (80%-85%), praline/gianduja, and enrobed nuts, plus chocolate and cream liqueurs.

### Semisweet Chocolate (50%-69% cacao)

This category presents strong, complex flavors with nutty, spicy, floral, earthy, fruity, and caramel nuances. The aftertaste remains balanced, not overly sweet.

Recommended Pairings: Same wines as bittersweet chocolate, with additional rationale:

- Fortified fruity wines like Banyuls and Ruby Port possess cocoa or chocolate notes alongside cherry, raspberry, or berry fruit. Non-vintage Ports feature softer tannins than vintage versions, making them superior chocolate companions.
- Beaujolais and Orange Muscat highlight fruity characteristics. While Orange Muscat represents a predictable choice, Beaujolais offers an unexpected pairing.
- Cabernet Sauvignon enhances fruity-peppery-grapey notes.
- Muscat complements fruit-filled dark chocolates and fruit truffles.
- Tawny Ports contribute nutty and tobacco/leather notes; vintage Ports risk overwhelming chocolate due to high sugar and alcohol.
- Zinfandel spiciness accentuates chocolate spicy nuances.

### Milk Chocolate

Higher sugar and lower chocolate liquor percentages, combined with milk content, yield a milder, sweeter product. Prominent flavors include brown sugar, cocoa, vanilla, honey, caramel, milk, cream, nutty, and malt.

Recommended Pairings:
- Hungarian Tokaji
- Mas Amiel Rouge & Vintage Blanc
- Muscat/Moscato
- Tawny Port

Pairing Logic:

Sweeter chocolate needs sweeter wine, or the wine may taste tart. Hungarian Tokaji dances beautifully with milk chocolate ganache. Muscat, a white dessert wine featuring peach and apricot flavors, proves popular. Tawny Port represents the strongest match with milk chocolate because its nutty nuances highlight milk chocolate nutty and caramel notes and enhance the overall chocolate flavor.

### White Chocolate

Made without chocolate liquor and thus technically not true chocolate, white chocolate combines cocoa butter, sugar, and milk solids. Sweet flavor notes include cream, milk, honey, vanilla, caramel, and fruit.

Recommended Pairings:
- Champagne
- Gewurztraminer
- Mas Amiel Vintage Blanc
- Muscat/Moscato
- Riesling

Detailed Guidance:

While Champagne pairing seems romantic, the acid in sparkling wines produces tart flavors when paired with the cacao in milk or dark chocolate. However, there is no cacao in white chocolate, so white is a fine choice. Select sweeter Champagne (sec or demi-sec) rather than bottles made for food pairing.

Gewurztraminer slight sweetness and lychee fruit create an exotic pairing. Mas Amiel Vintage Blanc specifically targets white chocolate. Muscat pairs better than other white dessert wines due to lower acidity. Riesling exotic sweetness that complements the vanilla, caramel and honey flavors of white chocolate works especially well, with Alsatian or German versions potentially offering unusual petrol notes.

### Ruby Chocolate

Introduced in 2018, ruby chocolate derives its natural rosy color from particular cacao beans.

Recommended Pairings:
- Belgian Kriek Beer
- Muscat/Moscato
- Rose Champagne

Character Notes: Ruby chocolate is naturally tart with berry fruit and citrus notes. Though creamy like other varieties, it contains no chocolaty flavors.

## Chocolate With Flavor Additions

### Caramel or Toffee

Recommended Pairings:
- Hungarian Tokaji
- Mas Amiel Rouge & Vintage Blanc
- Madeira
- Meursault
- Oloroso Sherry
- Sauternes
- Tawny Port

Pairing Rationale:

Hungarian Tokaji, featuring apricot, butter, and caramel notes, pairs with buttery salt caramels. Young Madeira (5-year) includes classic caramel and toffee flavors. Meursault from ripe years, with rich fruit and low acidity, complements the brown sugar and caramel flavors of the caramel or toffee as well as the cocoa flavors of chocolate.

Dry Oloroso Sherry nutty bouquet complements nuts in toffee, particularly salt caramels. Sauternes rich sweetness with honey and apricot notes complements caramel and toffee; the candy chewiness withstands the wine viscosity. Tawny Port enhances toffee nutty nuances and, to a lesser extent, caramel.

### Chestnut Creme

Recommended Pairing:
- Medium Dry Sherry

Also try with marrons glacees.

### Chiles (Habanero, etc.)

Recommended Pairings:
- Mas Amiel Rouge
- Ruby Port

A fruity, fortified red wine will pair well with the heat of chile-infused chocolate.

### Cinnamon & Ginger

Recommended Pairings:
- Mas Amiel Classique
- Zinfandel (or a dry red)

Two approaches accommodate spicy cinnamon and ginger: fortified, sweet red Mas Amiel, or spicy dry red Zinfandel. Sweet Late Harvest Zinfandel offers an almost Port-like alternative.

### Coconut

Recommended Pairings:
- Brachetto d Acqui
- Sauternes/Late Harvest Semillon

Brachetto d Acqui, a light ruby-colored sparkling dessert wine from Piedmont with fruit and rose aromas, works with nuts and coconut. Sauternes (Lafaurie-Peyraguey recommended) or Australian Late Harvest Semillon complement similarly.

### Coffee

(Espresso, mocha, coffee bean, and related flavors)

Recommended Pairings:
- Sherry (Oloroso or Cream)
- Coffee Liqueurs (Kahlua, Illy, Starbucks, Tia Maria)
- Chocolate and Cream Liqueurs

### Fruit

(Fruit-flavored truffles, chocolate cherries, chocolate-covered fruit, dipped/glazed/fresh fruit)

Recommended Pairings:
- Banyuls
- Cabernet Sauvignon
- Madeira
- Merlot
- Ruby Port

Wines with strong fruit notes suit fruit-accented chocolate. Banyuls cherry and berry flavors pair nicely. Cabernet Sauvignon enhances cherry-pepper-grape notes; its heavier tannins pair better with dark chocolate. Madeira traditionally complements figs and pairs beautifully with fig-filled or fig-dipped chocolate. Softer Merlot works well, particularly with milk chocolate. Ruby Port highlights cherry and chocolate flavors.

### Fruit Ganache & Fruit Creme

Recommended Pairings:
- Framboise Dessert Wine
- Orange Muscat
- Marsala
- Pinot Noir
- Port
- Rose Champagne

Bonnie Doon Framboise, described as an infusion of raspberries and made for chocolate pairing, works particularly well. Try Marsala or Vintage Port with citrus centers. A fruity Pinot Noir accents both the fruit and the fruitiness inherent in some fine couverture chocolate. With milder tannins, it also matches subtle creme fillings.

### Mint

Recommended Pairings:
- Cabernet Sauvignon
- Zinfandel
- Chocolate Mint Liqueur (Bailey Mint, Vandermint)

Cabernets and Zinfandels excel with chocolate mint cremes, mint thins, and mint bars, especially paired with dark chocolate. Some California versions feature pronounced minty nuances; wine merchants can direct to specific producers. For variety, try both minty and non-minty wines.

### Nuts

(Hazelnuts, almonds, praline)

Recommended Pairings:
- Brachetto d Acqui
- Cabernet Sauvignon
- Mas Amiel Rouge & Vintage Blanc
- Sherry
- Tawny Port

Brachetto d Acqui uniquely matches almonds and nuts. Cabernet Sauvignon brings out nutty accents and grapey flavors. Jurancon, with lime and clove aromas, harmonizes with pralines. Mas Amiel sweetness complements chocolate while highlighting nuts sweet qualities. Non-too-sweet Sherry works with almond-based chocolate. Ideally Pedro Ximinez (with almond aromas) or well-rounded Fino. Cream Sherry matches hazelnuts. Nutty Tawny Port is the perfect match for a box of chocolate-covered nuts. Lighter nuts like pistachio pair with Sauternes.

### Spiced Chocolate

(Bar or ganache)

Recommended Pairing:
- Jurancon

Jurancon lime and clove aromas match nicely with spice-flavored ganaches.

Recommended Pairing:
