I always feel it is appropriate to recommend something that will be extremely easy to drink, a wine that has slightly more sugar than the average wine. A wine that is fruity and lively with high, but balanced acid.

White wines-:

Muscat also knows as: muskat/moscato/moscatel. This grape is bursting with luscious grape and peach character, there will be sparkling and dessert version too! There are dry and sweet versions, with everything in between.

Riesling- This grape has very high acid and can be anything from bone dry to super sweet, but tends to have concentrated fruit character!

Pinot Grigio- Often referred to as “the wine for people who don't like wine”. This is a very high yielding grape with the ability to grow in many different environments, not always to the best quality however. Cheap, inexpensive styles can be somewhat diluted and inoffensive, making them very gluggable for most people, Pinot Grigio is arguably the most popular grape in the UK and Irish markets!

Red wines:

Pinot Noir- This grape is often light in body, with smooth tannins and a fruity character making it easy to drink for even those who do not like red wine. It can be a very complex and interesting wine when made with quality fruit and therefore is loved by both novices and connoisseurs.

Beaujolais- This is made from the gamay grape and tends to be super light and easy to drink, it can even be slightly chilled.

Primitivo- A grape from Puglia, southern Italy. Expect a full bodied red wine with luscious, jammy red fruit and a touch of liquorice or aniseed!

These should be worth exploring my friend!

I wouldn’t focus on any particular wine in and of itself, but rather how it pairs with food.

The right food and wine pairing can make both infinitely more enjoyable. And you don’t need to drink a lot of it, or even get a buzz. One glass with a meal or a cheese course will do ya just fine.

Many restaurants will offer wine pairings with meals, and you don’t have to drink it with every course. Your first glass is the most important. They say the palate becomes “trashed” very quickly, and the synergy of the food/wine pairings are less impactful the more glasses you drink.

So if you’re interested in dabbling in the world of wine, don’t just go out and buy a bottle of whatever and drink it straight. Go to a restaurant that serves food you love, and that can include dessert, and ask the waiter/sommelier to hook you up with a wine that blows your mind.
