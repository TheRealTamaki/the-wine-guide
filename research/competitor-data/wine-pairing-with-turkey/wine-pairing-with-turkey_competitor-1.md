## Quick Picks – Best Wines to Pair with Turkey

Here are the standout types of wine that pair well with turkey - the 17 types of wine that have risen to the top after many years of testing:

- **Top whites:** Dry Riesling, Chenin Blanc, Pinot Gris / Pinot Grigio
- **Top reds:** Beaujolais, Pinot Noir, Grenache based blends, Zinfandel
- **Rosé:** Dry, food-friendly rosé (such a Cerasuolo d’Abruzzo)
- **New for this year**: White Rhône style blend, skin-contact Gruner Veltliner

These wines work well with roast turkey and the Thanksgiving turkey dinner thanks to their weight, acidity and moderate tannins. Keep on reading for more details on what makes these wines work.

## New Pairings for This Year

A White Rhône-style blend was my favorite new pairing this year - specifically the **2024 Dineen Vineyards Condriesque**, a 50/50 mix of Roussanne and Viognier from Washington’s Yakima Valley.

[![turkey dinner with a bottle of Condriesque wine in the background.](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20width='1200'%20height='1200'%20viewBox='0%200%201200%201200'%3E%3C/svg%3E)](https://cookingchatfood.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/dineen-pairing-sq.jpg) _Roast turkey dinner paired with a White Rhône-style blend from Dineen Vineyards._

With notes of lemon peel, almond, and a touch of honey, it has enough body to complement roast turkey and potatoes while also working nicely with sweeter elements like cranberry sauce.

The wine's rounded texture and citrus-honey character helped it play well across the whole Thanksgiving plate, including Brussels sprouts with balsamic vinegar. It's an excellent choice if you want a fuller-bodied white that still has the lift to match turkey. _(You can check out more detailed tasting notes in my article [A White Rhone Style Blend for Thanksgiving](https://cookingchatfood.com/white-rhone-style-blend-for-thanksgiving/))_

I will have to do more experimenting, but from this recent test, I am inclined to think White Rhônes from France as well as their American counterparts could be good wine options for the holiday table.

### Skin-contact wines

Another recent winner from my testing was the **2022 Christina Gruner Veltliner ($20, 12% ABV)**, a skin-contact Gruner from Austria. This wine has peach fruit on the palate, and a chewy mouthfeel.

[![bottle of Christina Gruner Veltliner with a glass of the wine to the side of the bottle.](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20width='1200'%20height='1104'%20viewBox='0%200%201200%201104'%3E%3C/svg%3E)](https://cookingchatfood.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/11/Christina-gruner.jpg)

Sipping it on its own before dinner, I noticed the funkiness of it. But it paired pretty well with the range of foods on the Thanksgiving table, especially the smoky aspects of gravy and stuffing. The interesting label design celebrates helpful bugs! This is in keeping with this organic wine, that touts a natural winemaking approach.

I only tried this one skin-contact Grüner, but the style's mix of texture and freshness really worked with the turkey dinner. I'd say other skin-contact whites, aka “orange wines” have good potential to try for Thanksgiving, if you like wines in this category.

### Options that didn’t work well

During our most recent round of testing, we tried two red wines that didn’t work very well as pairings for the turkey dinner. Cabernet Franc, while being a f [ood-friendly option for many meals](https://cookingchatfood.com/cabernet-franc-food-pairing/), didn’t make a great match for the turkey.

A local Petite Pearl also seemed better suited for BBQ and other meaty meals, more so than the Thanksgiving dinner.

## Best wine with turkey – reliable options

OK, I have a lot to say about wines to pair with turkey. In this section, I describe some solid, reliable types of wine that go with turkey, along with some of the specific wines I’ve enjoyed. These wines go well with the turkey itself and tend to also pair with common side dishes for a turkey dinner.

### White wine and rosés

**Chenin Blanc** is a medium-bodied white wine grape that is versatile and food-friendly. France, particularly the Loire region, and South Africa, are good sources for Chenin Blanc. **[Vouvray](https://cookingchatfood.com/vouvray-wine/)** is a great type of Chenin Blanc to pick. This is an appellation within the Loire where the the white wine is made with Chenin Blanc.

[![The bottles of wine on a deck railing, with light snow covering the railing. Fallen leaves in the background. ](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20width='736'%20height='876'%20viewBox='0%200%20736%20876'%3E%3C/svg%3E)](https://cookingchatfood.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/11/Thanksgiving-wine-picks-a.jpg) _Champalou Vouvray, along with good Pinot Noir and Beaujolais options._

**Champalou Vouvray** is a reliable choice in this category.

**Pinot Gris/ Pinot Grigio** is another good choice in the white wine department if you pick a good quality bottle (there are cheap versions that give the grape a bad name). **Albino Armani** is an Italian producer with a great passion for Pinot Grigio; and can be a great choice. We also enjoyed the **Girlan Alto Adige Pinot Grigio** paired with a recent Thanksgiving meal.

[![Glass of Westport Rivers Pinot Gris alongside the bottle. ](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20width='1200'%20height='1314'%20viewBox='0%200%201200%201314'%3E%3C/svg%3E)](https://cookingchatfood.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/11/pinot-gris-westport.jpg) _Westport Rivers Pinot Gris is a good New England wine for Thanksgiving_

Pinot Gris from Oregon is a nice option for Thanksgiving if you want go for an American white wine. **Westport Rivers Pinot Gris** is a nice option if you’d like to feature a New England winery. It’s located about an hour from Plymouth.

**Full bodied rosés** can pair well with turkey and match the sides as well. We enjoyed a Cerasuolo d’Abruzzo for a recent Thanksgiving, but those can be a bit hard to find. The **Hoopes Rosé** is a very good one I’ve tried recently, made from Napa Cabernet. Other good recent rosés I’ve had include the **Alexander Valley Vineyards** **Dry Rosé of Sangiovese** ($18) and the **Famille Bougrier Rosé d’Anjou** ($12).

### Red wine pairings

**Pinot Noir** is definitely a go to red wine turkey! While I like to try other wines, there’s almost always Pinot Noir involved for Thanksgiving. Pinot tends to be lighter than other reds, and many have an earthiness that can work well with a variety of foods served with the turkey dinner.

As much as I like Burgundy, I tend to go for American Pinot Noir for Thanksgiving, both because it’s an American holiday and they tend to be more fruit forward. I’m going to start by highlighting two Pinots I tried in 2023 that would be a great choice for Thanksgiving.

[![A bottle of Sonoma-Cutrer Russian River Pinot Noir with a glass of the red wine to the right of the bottle.](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20width='1200'%20height='1200'%20viewBox='0%200%201200%201200'%3E%3C/svg%3E)](https://cookingchatfood.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/11/sonoma-cutrer-pinot-noir.jpg) _This Sonoma-Cutrer Pinot Noir is an excellent Thanksgiving wine pick_

The **2021 Sonoma-Cutrer Russian River Valley Pinot Noir** ($36, sample) would be a great wine to pair with turkey. It has dark red fruit on the nose. On the palate, cherries and a touch of baking spices. A very good wine, with a reasonable price point for the holiday feast.

Staying in the Russian River Valley, the **2018 Cameron Hughes Lot 947** ($19, 14.5% ABV, sample) is a great value to consider for Thanksgiving.

[![Cameron Hughes Pinot Noir bottle with a glass of the red wine alongside it.](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20width='1200'%20height='1200'%20viewBox='0%200%201200%201200'%3E%3C/svg%3E)](https://cookingchatfood.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/11/cameron-hughes-pinot.jpg)

This fruit forward, easy drinking Pinot is sure to please. [Cameron Hughes](https://chwine.com/) has a model where they don’t own vineyards, but rather seek to source wine from top estates and negotiate a good price for it.

**Updated Pinot Pick:** I tried the **2023 Domaine de la Cras Bourgogne ‘Coteaux de Dijon’** ($50, 12.5% ABV) the other day at Berman’s in Lexington, MA, and eagerly grabbed a bottle at 20% off the recommended price. I haven’t tested this yet with turkey, but with tastingnotes notes of _“cranberry, shelled almond, baking spices_” it sure sounds like a winner!

Some moderately priced (i.e. around $20 or less) Pinot options include **Ritual Pinot Noir** (Chile – I wrote about [Ritual here](https://cookingchatfood.com/asian-grilled-pork-tenderloin/)), **Evolution** from Sokol Blosser (Willamette Valley) and **Banshee** from Sonoma County. The **2020 Pullus Pinot Noir** ($17) from Slovenia is another good option.

[![A glass of Evolution Pinot Noir with the bottle alongside it, tilted so you can see the label.](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20width='1200'%20height='1152'%20viewBox='0%200%201200%201152'%3E%3C/svg%3E)](https://cookingchatfood.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/evolution-pinot.jpg) _Evolution Pinot Willamette Valley Pinot Noir is a good, reasonably priced option._

If you’re up for considering a higher priced Pinot Noir, some wines worthy of the splurge that work well with turkey include **Eric Kent Russian River Valley Pinot Noir**, **Marimar Estate** Pinot from the Sonoma Coast and **Sokol Blosser Big Tree Block Pinot Noir** from Oregon’s Dundee Hills. (Sokol Blosser has other good options but I happened to give that bottle a rare for me “5” rating on Vivino!).

**Beaujolais:** I’m not a fan of the young Beaujolais Nouveau that comes out in November. But Beaujolais Villages and Cru Beaujolais are good quality French red wines made from the Gamay, and are very food friendly. The earthy Beaujolais wines can be a good partner with turkey and other savory dishes on the table.

We covered some good [Beaujolais pairings](https://cookingchatfood.com/food-friendly-red-wine-from-beaujolais/) in a past article, and you can get more background on the region [in this article](https://flatiron-wines.com/blogs/the-latest/what-is-the-difference-between-beaujolais-beaujolais-village-and-the-beaujolais-crus). The 2015 **Brouilly Tradition** from Domaine de Briante is one we particularly enjoyed; I’m going to have to seek out a newer vintage for this Thanksgiving! And the **Domaine de la Gran’Cour Fleurie Lieu-dit Champagne Vielles Vignes ($42)** is a very elegant expression of Gamay that I tried a Pamplemousse.

**Zinfandel** is another classic wine choice for Thanksgiving. It has often been referred to as an “All American Grape”, thus making it a natural choice for the American holiday. We now know it is genetically similar to the Italian grape Primitivo, and [this article covers some of the Zinfandel history](https://vinepair.com/wine-geekly/zinfandel-the-all-american-grape-comes-from-kastela-croatia/) showing its European roots.

The **Marietta Román Zinfandel**($25) from Sonoma County is a very good Zinfandel I’ve enjoyed recently. **Pedroncelli** is a solid choice for reasonably priced Zinfandel. **Ridge** is a well-known Zinfandel producer if you are up for spending more.

## Creative turkey wine pairings

OK, now that we’ve covered 6 reliable turkey wine pairings, let’s now move to some additional creative ideas.

**Gewurztraminer**: Chatting about Thanksgiving wine pairings on social media, I found a few touting the merits of **Gewurztraminer** as a pairing choice. Thus it was timely that my sister brought a bottle to a recent Thanksgiving dinner. Anne de K Gewurzttraminer ($18) from Alsace. Lighter than the Pinot Gris, the spice element was good with things like stuffing and gravy that had notable amounts of black pepper in the preparation.

**Furmint,** a dry white wine from Hungary, can be another great choice for Thanksgiving. We have tested the **Royal Tokaji Furmint,** with a smoky fruit aspect on the palate, that worked nicely with the turkey gravy. **Sauska** is another Furmint I’ve enjoyed, and **Oddity** is another one that is available fairly often.

**Riesling** is known for its versatility and food friendliness, so it’s no surprise to see it mentioned as a turkey wine pairing option. Alsace is a great region for Riesling.

[![Glass of Riesling white wine with the bottle to the left of the glass.](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20width='1200'%20height='1388'%20viewBox='0%200%201200%201388'%3E%3C/svg%3E)](https://cookingchatfood.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/11/paul-blank-riesling.jpg) _Riesling is a good friendly wine, and can pair well with a turkey dinner._

The **Paul Blank Riesling Classique** ($22, 12.5% ABV) is a good one I’ve tried recently. If you want to go with an American Riesling, the Finger Lakes is a great region to choose. Washington state also can be a good source for Riesling.

**Albariño** definitely falls into the creative category when considering Thanksgiving wine pairings! Some years ago we tried the **Benito Santos Albarino**($20, 13% ABV), and noted that this crisp Albariño worked quite well, especially with things like the squash and cranberry sauce.

[![Three bottles of wine lined up on a deck railing - a Pinot Noir, a Cinsault and an Rkatsiteli.](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20width='1200'%20height='1290'%20viewBox='0%200%201200%201290'%3E%3C/svg%3E)](https://cookingchatfood.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/11/thansgiving-2023.jpg) _A recent lineup of Thanksgiving wines we tested_

**Rkatsiteli** is another interesting white wine choice to go with turkey. This is a high acidity white wine from Georgia that can be food friendly. We tested the **2021 Marnaveli Rkatsiteli**($16, 13% ABV). With a citrusy flavor profile and notes of almond, the Rkatz is another option for your Thanksgiving dinner.

**Viogner** is an aromatic white wine you might also consider for Thanksgiving. We have tested a 100% Viogner, the **2020 Vignoble Belot Pays D'Oc ($15)** that worked well with the turkey as well as the side dishes.

**Rhone Reds** are definitely worthy of consideration to pair with turkey. Especially from under the radar Cru regions like Lirac and Rasteau. These are high quality GSM blends (Grenache / Syrah / Mourvedre). We had the **2016** **Domaine Coudoulis Lirac Red**(appx $15, 15% ABV) as one of several bottles opened for a past Thanksgiving. After having had a fair amount of food and wine already, the bold flavors of this wine re-awakened my palate. Higher alcohol than you'd expect would work well with all the foods on the table, but a well-made wine can surprise!

## Tips for pairing wine with turkey

[![A Thanksgiving spread, with turkey, gravy, cranberry sauce and other classic sides. Glass of white and red wines in the background.](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20width='1200'%20height='1527'%20viewBox='0%200%201200%201527'%3E%3C/svg%3E)](https://cookingchatfood.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/11/thanksgiving-2025-spread.jpg) _Yes, I take wine pairing notes as we eat our Thanksgiving dinner!_

So I’ve given you about a dozen types of wine that work very well with turkey, based on my tasting over the years. Let me also share some general tips that can help you try other types of wine, and find specific bottles of wine that will work for you.

**Fruit forward wines** tend to go well with turkey and the various sides often served with turkey.

- **Fruit forward wines** tend to go well with turkey and the various sides often served with turkey.
- **Lower alcohol wine** s are generally more food friendly, and this can be especially helpful when you are pairing wine for a big Thanksgiving feast.
- **Avoid wine with a lot of tannins** that tend to clash with turkey and the sides. Save that big Napa Cabernet or Bordeaux for another day – maybe your holiday [spoon roast](https://cookingchatfood.com/spoon-roast-horseradish-cream/)!
- **Match flavor elements on the table** with the tasting notes of the wine. For instance, the sage we have included in our roasted turkey breast recipe matches the garrigue notes of the wines we have paired with it.
- **Experiment** with a variety wines, and see what you like best! An article like this one can give you some ideas, but there’s nothing like testing your own taste buds! Thanksgiving can be a great chance to experiment, if you have a group of wine drinkers at the table. Open several bottles, take a modest pour of each, and see what works best for you.

## Best wine with turkey – Frequently Asked Questions

**Red or white wine with turkey?**

The quick answer is that both red and white wines can work with turkey! We have tested a variety options of both types that can work. My personal preference is to pair a red wine if I’m focused mostly on the savory turkey and gravy. But white wines can work very well with many of the classic Thanksgiving side dishes, so white wines can be a good choice, too.

**What kind of wine is best with turkey?**

Chenin Blanc, Pinot Gris / Pinot Grigio, Pinot Noir and Beaujolais are some of the best wines to serve with turkey. This Cooking Chat article has many more ideas of wines that can go with turkey, too!

**Can you serve red wine with turkey?**

Yes, a number of red wines can go well with turkey. Red wines that are light to medium body, and low in tannins, are the best to serve with turkey.

**Are there wines that are not good to have with turkey?**

Stay away from wines with a lot of tannins. Also, wines with high alcohol content can clash with many of the dishes served along with a turkey dinner.

**What wine goes with Thanksgiving dinner?**

If you are thinking about a wine that goes with all of the Thanksgiving sides as well as the turkey, in our testing we have found that white wines can work very well. Chenin Blanc, Pinot Grigio and Riesling are some good choices. If you would prefer red, Pinot Noir is one of the most versatile choices to go with Thanksgiving dinner and all the dishes involved.

While I do often fall back to Pinot Noir with the fairly traditional turkey we eat on Thanksgiving, I like to experiment, too.

And I especially like food and wine pairings where both elements grow together in the same region. That concept worked very nicely when we originally made this **Garrigue Herb Roasted Turkey Breast with a Wine from Languedoc** pairing we came up with for the November 2015 Thanksgiving edition of Wine Pairing Weekend.

## Tips for making this recipe

Roast turkey breast can be a nice option if you are just cooking for your immediate family or a smaller gathering.

For this recipe, I made an herb butter with sage, rosemary and thyme — classic garrigue herbs. I rubbed it all over the turkey before roasting, and the house smelled great for the next couple of hours.

[![Garrigue Herb Roasted Turkey Breast comes out a nice golden brown.](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20width='550'%20height='393'%20viewBox='0%200%20550%20393'%3E%3C/svg%3E)](https://cookingchatfood.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/11/garrigue-roasted-turkey-breast.jpg)

Here are some tips for making Herb Roast Turkey Breast. For the full detailed instructions, [click here to jump down to the recipe card](https://cookingchatfood.com/roasted-turkey-breast-with-wine/#garrigue-recipe).

**Start by making the herb butter.** Take the butter out about 30 minutes prior to using it to soften.

[![making her butter for turkey in a white bowl.](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20width='500'%20height='400'%20viewBox='0%200%20500%20400'%3E%3C/svg%3E)](https://cookingchatfood.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/11/herb-butter-turkey.jpeg)

The recipe calls for fresh rosemary, thyme and sage (garrigue herbs). But you can use dried herbs if you like; using at least one fresh herb is idea.

**Rub the herb butter** all over the turkey breast. In addition, separate a bit of the skin from the turkey flesh and slip a bit of the butter underneath the skin.

![turkey breast with herb butter, ready to go into oven.](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20width='500'%20height='280'%20viewBox='0%200%20500%20280'%3E%3C/svg%3E)

I used a 325 degree setting in a convection oven. If you don’t have convection, adjust the heat to 350 degrees conventional.

**Place the turkey skin side up** so the fat from the skin drips into the meat.

**Roasting times vary** based on your oven and the size of the turkey. Roasting times for a 3 to 5 lb turkey breast are typically given as 90 to 120 minutes. Check at 90 minutes, temperature in middle should be 170 degrees. Our 4 pound turkey breast was ready at the 90 minute mark.

The first time I made the gravy, I had some liquid from reconstituting porcini mushrooms and used ¼ cup of that along with the chicken broth. If you have something like that on hand, it adds nice depth of flavor. But it is fine to just use chicken stock if that is what you have.

If you wind up with leftovers, two of our favorite recipes are this [Turkey and Butternut Squash Stew](https://cookingchatfood.com/turkey-butternut-squash-stew/) and our [Leftover Turkey Pasta with Mushrooms](https://cookingchatfood.com/leftover-turkey-pasta/).

## Wine pairings for roast turkey recipe

Making this Herb Roasted Turkey Breast again this year gave me a chance to test out some new wine pairings. For many years, I often went for red wine almost exclusively with the Thanksgiving meal. But more recently I’ve been bringing some whites to the table. I did a red or wine for turkey comparison this time; here are the results.

The **2017 Domaine Brussett La Bastide** ($21, 13.5% ABV–sample bottle provided) from the Rasteau AOC has rustic qualities that made it a nice match for this turkey recipe, especially with its herbal qualities. For more on the Rasteau AOC in the Rhone Valley, [see this article](https://cookingchatfood.com/chicken-lentil-stew/) we did on the subject.

![Anne Amie Pinot Gris paired with turkey dinner.](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20width='550'%20height='550'%20viewBox='0%200%20550%20550'%3E%3C/svg%3E)

Our white option for this turkey dinner was the **2018 Anne Amie Vineyards Willamette Valley Pinot Gris**($20, 13.3% ABV). Honeysuckle on the nose, on the palate honeydew melon and lychee; underling minerality and a light touch of honey.  A very good wine.

Chenin Blanc, the grape varietal used for Vouvray, is another Thanksgiving dinner friendly white wine I’ve tested out recently. And you can’t go wrong with a good Pinot Noir if you are in the mood for red wine!

You can also enjoy this turkey recipe with a good American Pinot Noir! That was what we did the last time we made this recipe.

### A Languedoc wine pairing

For the original making of this recipe, I opened the **2013 Domaine Terres Georges Minervois Et Cetera** ($14 — I was given a sample bottle), a blend of Carrignan (40%), Grenache (40%) and Syrah (20 %). Minervois is an AOC in the Languedoc region of France.

![Domaine Terres Georges is a very nice wine from the Languedoc, pairs well with herb roasted turkey.](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20width='680'%20height='1175'%20viewBox='0%200%20680%201175'%3E%3C/svg%3E)

Sure enough, I detected earthiness and fresh herbs on the nose. Taste of plum fruit, lavender and stone. Those garrigue herbs really come through, and make for a great pairing with the herb roasted turkey breast. This pairing is definitely a winner, and one I’d recommend to someone looking for something a little different for Thanksgiving.

_This Herb Roasted Turkey Breast is an easy recipe for creating moist, flavorful turkey. A great option for a group of eight or so gathering for Thanksgiving or other occasions._

- **Author:** [Cooking Chat](https://cookingchatfood.com/about/)
- **Prep Time:** 15 mins
- **Cook Time:** 110 mins
- **Total Time:** 2 hours 5 mins
- **Yield:** 10 servings 1x
- **Category:** Main
- **Method:** Roasting
- **Cuisine:** French

### Ingredients

Scale1x2x3x

- 2 cloves garlic, minced and divided
- 5 tbsp butter (1 reserved), left out of fridge for 20 minutes or so to soften
- 1 sprig rosemary, finely chopped
- 4 sage leaves, chopped
- 1 sprig thyme, chopped
- salt and pepper to taste
- 4 lbs boneless turkey breast
- 2 tbsp shallot
- 1 ¾ cup chicken stock (swap in ¼ cup reserved porcini liquid or other such rich broth if you have it)
- sliver lemon peel
- pinch thyme
- pinch lavender or substitute 2 basil leaves, chopped
- 2 sage leaves

Cook Mode
Prevent your screen from going dark

### Instructions

01. Preheat oven to 325 degrees convection setting (350 conventional).
02. Make the herb butter: place 4 tablespoons of the softened butter in a small bowl. Add ½ of the minced garlic, the rosemary, 4 chopped sage leaves, the chopped thyme, and salt and pepper to taste. Stir to combine well, and set aside.
03. With a clean paper towel, pat the turkey breast dry upon removing it from packaging. Set the turkey out on a clean work surface, and rub the butter all over the turkey. Put more on the skin side, work some under the skin if you can.
04. Place the turkey breast on a roasting rack, in a roasting pan. Place in the oven and roast. Roasting times vary based on your oven and the size of the turkey. Roasting times for a 3 to 5 lb turkey breast typically given as 90 to 120 minutes. Check at 90 minutes, temperature in middle should be 170 degrees. Ours took about 100 minutes in a convection oven to be ready. When done, remove the turkey breast from oven and let rest for 10 minutes on a large platter.
05. Begin making the light gravy after the turkey has been cooking for 30 minutes or so. Heat the remaining tablespoon of butter in a sauce pan. Add the shallot, and cook 5 minutes until softened.
06. Add the chicken stock to the sauce pan, and bring to a boil. Then reduce heat to simmer.
07. Add the lemon peel, pinch of thyme, lavender and 2 sage leaves. Simmer the stock until it is reduced by about ½. Keep warm.
08. After you’ve put together the gravy ingredients and they are simmering together, pull out a quarter cup and use that to baste the turkey about halfway through the cooking time.
09. When the turkey is cooked, strain the broth through a sieve, with a bowl underneath to catch and reserve the liquid. Pour the liquid into the roasting pan. Place the pan on the stove top, and bring the liquid to a boil. Reduce to a simmer, and stir frequently, scraping the bottom of the pan to incorporate the bits of turkey fat that dropped to the bottom of the pan during the roasting. Simmer for a few minutes, then pour the liquid into a serving bowl.
10. Slice the the turkey, and plate a few pieces along with your side dishes. Serve with the light gravy and a bottle of red wine!

### Notes

- We previously included rinsing the turkey breast before cooking. We now follow the [USDA guidelines to wash your hands, not the turkey](https://www.usda.gov/media/blog/2013/11/21/wash-or-not-wash-your-turkey)!
- Take the butter out about 30 minutes prior to cooking to soften it.
- If you don’t have a convection oven, increase the temperature to 350 degrees for a conventional setting. Cooking time will be similar to convection with that adjustment.
- The turkey breast should register 165 degrees upon inserting a meat thermometer into the thickest part of the breast meat.
