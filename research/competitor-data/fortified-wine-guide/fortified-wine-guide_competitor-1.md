## **Fortified Wine at a Glance**    **Alcohol by Volume (abv):** 15-20%    **Flavor:** This varies and can be dry, sweet, fruity, bitter and/or nutty.    **Common Varietals:** Port, Sherry, Marsala, Madeira    **Food Pairings:** Cheeses, nuts, dried fruit, chocolate and cream-based desserts.    **Serving Temperature:** 57 to 60 degrees for Port or Fino Sherry; 66 degrees for Madeira or Vintage Port.    **Glassware:** A small, stemmed glass known as a copita. Look for something with a tall bowl and narrow opening to retain aromas while releasing the alcohol.

From Spanish [Sherries](https://www.wineenthusiast.com/gallery/everything-you-need-to-know-about-sherry/) to Australian Rutherglen Muscats, fortified wines vary in color, flavor, origin and sweetness. But all have one thing in common: fortification. Below, we explain the most common styles and histories of fortified wine.

## **What Is a Fortified Wine?**

Fortification means that a grape spirit has been added to the wine either during or after fermentation. It’s a technique used to increase alcohol content and stop fermentation. The process was popularized by the English in the late 17th century to stabilize and preserve wines for long sea voyages. Prior to the development of fortification, many of these wines were made as still, unfortified wines.

Many decisions, like at what point during fermentation a wine is fortified and how it’s matured, create a diverse array of bottlings.

Fortified wine isn’t to be confused with [aromatized wine](https://www.wineenthusiast.com/basics/drinks-terms-defined/aromatized-wine/), a style in which herbs and botanicals have been infused into a wine. That said, a wine can be both aromatized and fortified. Vermouth is the most common example of this combined style.

## **What Is Sherry?**

All Sherry hails from hot, dry southern Spain, centered in [the Sherry Triangle towns](https://www.wineenthusiast.com/2016/08/12/andalucia-and-the-sherry-triangle/) of Jerez de la Frontera, Sanlúcar de Barrameda and El Puerto de Santa María.

While the low-acid, white [Palomino](https://www.wineenthusiast.com/varietals/palomino/) grape dominates the region, it’s often supplemented by aromatic Moscatel (also known as [Muscat of Alexandria](https://www.wineenthusiast.com/varietals/muscat-of-alexandria/)) and robust [Pedro Ximénez](https://www.wineenthusiast.com/varietals/pedro-ximenez/). In general, fermentation takes place in neutral stainless-steel tanks, followed by extended aging in neutral barrels.

There are various styles of Sherry, but dry Sherries can be classified largely into two categories: those aged under a veil of yeast called flor, which includes fino and manzanilla, and ones matured with oxygen contact, like oloroso. Some, like amontillado and palo cortado, are “hybrid” styles that undergo both aging techniques.

Sweet Sherries are the result of different winemaking decisions. Naturally sweet Sherry like Pedro Ximénez and Moscatel are made from super-concentrated, dried grapes with sugar levels so high that fermentation doesn’t finish before the alcohol is added. They’re fortified to 15 or 16% abv.

Pale cream and cream Sherries are usually fermented until dry, then fortified and sweetened.

## **What Is Port?**

[Port](https://www.wineenthusiast.com/culture/wine/port-essential-guide/) is a sweet, fortified wine made from grapes grown on the steep slopes of Portugal’s Douro Valley. The warm, dry conditions make powerful, ripe red wines, though white grapes are grown as well.

Unlike Sherry, Port is often the result of a blend of multiple grape varieties, often from different vineyard sites. The most prominent ones used to make Port include Touriga Nacional, Touriga Franca, Tinta Barroca, Tinto Cão and Tinta Roriz.

Port is always sweet, though the exact level of sweetness depends on a house’s style. The quality and flavor of the grape spirit that’s added also matters, since a fair amount needs to be blended in to achieve Port’s typical 19-22% abv.

In addition to [white](https://www.wineenthusiast.com/2020/01/07/guide-white-port-wine/) and rosé styles, Port can be split into two categories. Tawny Ports are aged with oxygen, while ruby Ports are aged for two to three years in wood, cement or stainless steel prior to bottling.

If you see something labeled “ [Vintage Port](https://www.wineenthusiast.com/culture/wine/eight-vintage-ports-guide/)” it means that the bottle was made from a single year’s exceptional harvest. These ports are meant to age in the bottle.

![Vineyards on Madeira island](https://www.wineenthusiast.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/Madeira_GettyImages-1139127430_1920x1280.jpg)Vineyards on Madeira Island, Portugal/Getty

## **What Is Madeira?**

Made on the subtropical Portuguese island of the same name, [Madeira](https://www.wineenthusiast.com/2019/04/25/tasting-a-madeira-that-stood-the-test-of-time/) can vary in style based on grape variety and intended quality. About 85% of Madeira is made using the high-yielding red grape Tinta Negra. But the [best Madeira wines](https://www.wineenthusiast.com/culture/wine/tasting-a-madeira-that-stood-the-test-of-time/) are made generally from the island’s four white varieties: Sercial, Verdelho, Boal and Malvasia.

When a wine is labeled by variety, it can indicate the sweetness level.

For example, high-acid Sercial tends to be fortified later in the fermentation process, which makes a relatively drier wine. Meanwhile, high-sugar Malvasia tends to be fortified earlier to make a sweet wine. The added spirit is 96% abv, so just a little is needed to reach Madeira’s 17-18% abv.

## **What Is Marsala?**

[Marsala](https://www.wineenthusiast.com/culture/wine/marsala-wine/) is among the world’s historic wines, first fortified in 1773. Though commercialization in the past century resulted in a decline in quality, some producers in [western Sicily](https://www.wineenthusiast.com/2019/04/16/beginners-guide-to-the-wines-of-sicily/) have revived traditional, high-caliber Marsala.

With the exception of the less common rubino style, which uses red grapes, oro (golden) or lower-quality ambra (amber) Marsala is typically made from [Grillo](https://www.wineenthusiast.com/2016/08/30/grillo-mania/), [Inzolia](https://www.wineenthusiast.com/varietals/inzolia/) and [Catarratto](https://www.wineenthusiast.com/varietals/catarratto/). The wine may be fortified to 17% or 18% abv at any point during fermentation and ranges from dry secco (with up to 40 grams per liter (g/L) of residual sugar) to sweet dolce (with more than 100 g/L sugar).

Quality Marsala is aged in oak or cherry wood barrels. These wines are largely non-vintage and may be classified according to length of maturation. The range spans from fine, which must be aged for just one year, to vergine, which is dry and aged for at least five years.

## **What Are Vins Doux Naturels?**

France’s fortified wines, made in [Languedoc-Roussillon](https://www.wineenthusiast.com/region/languedoc-roussillon/) and [the Southern Rhône](https://www.wineenthusiast.com/2018/12/13/guide-wines-southern-rhone-valley/), are made largely from Muscat Blanc à Petits Grains, Muscat of Alexandria or Grenache.

Though they vary stylistically by region, vins doux naturels (VDN) are always sweet and fortified with a 95-96% abv grape spirit before fermentation is halted.

More often, these wines are unaged and youthful like Muscat de Rivesaltes, which features fresh stone, citrus and tropical fruit with floral and honeyed characteristics.

Red VDNs often are deep in color and well-structured. But depending on how they’re aged, they may range from juicy and deeply fruited to complex with dried fruit notes. [Banyuls](https://www.wineenthusiast.com/2018/03/15/banyuls-ultimate-dessert-wine/), Maury and Rivesaltes are all common regions for red VDNs.

## **What is Vermouth?**

[Vermouth](https://www.wineenthusiast.com/culture/vermouth-style-guide/) is a fortified, aromatized wine that was originally made for medicinal purposes. The common botanical flavors found in Vermouth include wormwood, orange peel, juniper, star anise and angelica root, with each brand having its own proprietary mix. Vermouth can be sweet or dry.

## **How to Shop for Fortified Wine**

Fortified wines can vary so much based on producer and style. A knowledgeable [local wine shop](https://www.wineenthusiast.com/culture/travel/the-best-neighborhood-wine-shops-across-the-u-s/) will be your best bet for purchasing bottles.

## **How to Cook with Fortified Wine**

An underappreciated style, fortified wines are especially good for [cooking](https://www.wineenthusiast.com/recipes/food-recipes/cooking-with-fortified-wines/). Due to their high alcohol content, they can stand up to heat well while retaining their flavors. Port, Sherry and Marsala are excellent in sauces and dressings.

Some of the most beloved recipes made with fortified wine include [Chicken Marsala](https://www.wineenthusiast.com/recipe/classic-chicken-marsala/) and [mushrooms braised in Madeira](https://www.wineenthusiast.com/recipe/how-to-make-madeira-mushrooms/).

## **How to Make Cocktails with Fortified Wine**

One of the best ways to enjoy a fortified wine is in a [cocktail](https://www.wineenthusiast.com/recipes/cocktail-recipes/fortified-wine-cocktails/). Use it as a base or to balance out higher abv spirits. Sweeter fortified wines, like Pedro Ximenez Sherry or ruby Port can replace syrups. Vermouth is one of the most [beloved cocktail ingredients](https://www.wineenthusiast.com/culture/spirits/best-vermouth-cocktails-bartender/).

Some of our favorite cocktail recipes with fortified wine include the [Madeira Flip](https://www.wineenthusiast.com/recipe/madeira-flip-cocktail-recipe/), [Colony Cobbler](https://www.wineenthusiast.com/recipe/colony-cobbler-sherry-cocktail/) and [Mezcal-Port Negroni](https://www.wineenthusiast.com/recipe/mezcal-port-negroni-cocktail/).
