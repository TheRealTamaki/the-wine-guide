# 5 Main Types of Dessert Wine

Dessert wines are meant to be enjoyed in small glasses and treasured like a glass of Scotch.

Most dessert wines can be categorized into 5 styles: Sparkling, Light & Sweet, Rich & Sweet, Sweet Red and Fortified.

## Types of Dessert Wines

- Sparkling Dessert Wine
- Lightly Sweet Dessert Wine
- Richly Sweet Dessert Wine
- Sweet Red Wine
- Fortified Wine

## A Guide to Dessert Wines

Sweet wine comes from extra-sweet grapes. To make a sweet wine, the fermentation is stopped before the yeast converts all grape sugars into alcohol. There are several ways to stop fermentations, including super-cooling the wine or adding brandy to it. The result is a rich wine sweetened with natural grape sugars.

There are hundreds of different types of dessert wines available in the market, but most fall into 5 main styles. This guide outlines the 5 styles and includes examples of each.

## Sparkling Dessert Wine

The carbonation and high acidity in sparkling wine makes it taste less sweet than it actually is. Certain grape varieties smell sweeter than others. This tricks our brain into thinking they taste sweeter too. For instance, a Demi-Sec Moscato tastes sweeter than a Demi-Sec Champagne even though both may have the same amount of sugar.

When looking for sweet dessert wine Champagnes and other sparklers, keep your eyes peeled for these words on the label:

- Demi-Sec (means "off-dry" in French)
- Amabile ("slightly sweet" in Italian)
- Semi Secco ("off-dry" in Italian)
- Doux ("sweet" in French)
- Dolce / Dulce ("sweet" in Italian / Spanish)
- Moelleux ("sweet" for some French wines)

## Lightly-Sweet Dessert Wine

Lightly sweet wines are refreshingly sweet; perfect for a warm afternoon. Many of these sweet wines pair well with spicy foods like Indian or Southeast Asian cuisine. Lightly-sweet wines are best enjoyed as close to the vintage date as possible, save for rare examples, like German Riesling, which age quite well.

Expect these wines to be exploding with fruit flavors and well suited for fruit-based and vanilla-driven desserts.

- Gewurztraminer: A highly floral wine with moderate alcohol that's commonly found in Alsace, Alto-Adige (Italy), California and New Zealand.
- Riesling: Available in both dry styles as well as sweeter styles more commonly available from Germany. A wine with high natural acidity which helps cut the sweet taste.
- Muller-Thurgau: A less common variety also from Germany and found in parts of Oregon that has floral aromas with slightly lighter acidity.
- Chenin Blanc: Commonly made in a sweeter style in the US and produced in large amounts in South Africa and the Loire Valley of France.
- Viognier: For the most part, not sweet. However, as an aromatic grape variety, occasionally found in a fruit-driven style smelling of peaches and perfume.

## Richly Sweet Dessert Wine

Richly sweet wines are made with the highest quality grapes in an unfortified style. Many of these wines can age 50+ years because sweetness and acidity preserve their fresh flavor. Some of these wines are historically important including Hungarian Tokaji which was loved by the Tzars of Russia; South African Constantia which was an obsession of the Dutch and English; and French Sauternes which was loved by Americans in the early 1800s.

There are several ways to produce richly sweet dessert wines.

### Late Harvest

Late harvest means exactly what it's called. As grapes hang on the vine longer in the season they become even sweeter and more raisinated, resulting in grapes with concentrated sweetness. In Alsace, late harvest is called "Vendage Tardive," and in Germany it's referred to as "Spatelse."

Any grape left on the vine can be used for late harvest wines. Popular grapes include Chenin Blanc, Semillon, and Riesling.

### Noble Rot

Noble rot is a type of spore called Botrytis cinerea that eats fruits and vegetables. While it sounds disgusting, noble rot adds unique flavors of ginger, saffron, and honey to sweet wines.

- Sauternais: A group of French Appellations in and around Bordeaux including Sauternes, Barsac, Cadillac, and Monbazillac that use Semillon and Sauvignon Blanc.
- Tokaji: A wine from Hungary made with Furmint grapes. Tokaji Aszu is a wine made with botrytis-affected grapes.
- Auslese, BA, and TBA Riesling: In the German Pradikat system, Auslese is the first level with a higher proportion of botrytis-affected grapes.

### Straw Mat (Passito)

Grapes are laid out on straw mats to raisinate prior to winemaking.

- Italian Vin Santo: Made with Trebbiano and Malvasia grapes and has rich nutty date-like flavors.
- Italian Passito: Another straw wine made with several different kinds of grapes.
- Greek Straw Wines: Greece produces Vinsanto, made with Assyrtiko grapes; Samos is made from Muscat grapes; and Commandaria is a sweet wine from Cyprus.
- German Strohwein/Austrian Schilfwein: Increasingly rare sweet wines made from Muscat and Zweigelt grapes.
- French Vin de Paille: Most notably from the Jura region of France, produced using Chardonnay and Savagnin grapes.

### Ice Wine (Eiswein)

True ice wine is extremely rare and expensive. It only occurs in bizarre years when a vineyard freezes, and ice wine must be harvested and pressed while grapes are still frozen.

The largest ice wine producer in the world is Canada. Ice wines are also found in Germany, Austria, and Switzerland.

Most ice wines are made with Riesling or Vidal grapes, although anything can be used. They tend to be honeyed and richly sweet, similar to a noble rot wine.

## Sweet Red Wine

Sweet reds are on decline except for cheap commercial production. However, there are still a few well-made historically interesting sweet reds worth trying. The majority are from Italy.

- Lambrusco: A region producing a refreshing bubbly wine in both dry and sweet styles.
- Brachetto d'Acqui: A still and bubbly red or rose wine from Piedmont. Famous for its floral and strawberry aromas.
- Schiava: A rare variety from Alto-Adige that smells sweetly of raspberry and cotton candy.
- Freisa: Once one of the great red varieties of Piedmont, related to Nebbiolo with lighter tannins.
- Recioto della Valpolicella: Made in the same painstaking process as Amarone, Recioto della Valpolicella is lush, bold and rich.
- Late-Harvest Red Wines: Many red dessert wines in the US made with Zinfandel, Mourvedre, Malbec and Petite Sirah.

## Fortified Wine

Fortified wines are made when grape brandy is added to a wine. Most fortified wines are higher in alcohol content (about 17-20% ABV) and have a longer shelf life after they are opened.

### Port

Port wine is made in the Northern part of Portugal along the Douro river. These rare sweet red wines are made with dozens of Portuguese traditional grapes including Touriga Nacional, Touriga Franca, and Tinta Roriz.

Styles:
- Ruby and Crusted Port (sweet): An introductory style of Port wine.
- Vintage and LBV Port (sweet): Made in the same style, LBV are designed to be enjoyed in their youth and vintage Ports are meant to be aged 20-50 years.
- Tawny Port (very sweet): Aged in large wooden casks and smaller wooden barrels. The longer the Tawny Port ages, the more nutty and figgy it becomes.
- Port-Style Wines / Vin Doux Naturel (sweet): Port can only be made in Portugal although many producers make port-style wines.

### Sherry

Sherry comes from Andalusia, Spain. Made using Palomino, Pedro Ximenez, and Moscatel grapes. Wines are produced using varying amounts of the three grapes and are purposefully oxidized.

Styles:
- Fino (dry): The lightest and most dry of all the Sherries.
- Manzanilla (dry): A specific style of Fino Sherry.
- Palo Cortado (dry): A slightly richer style of sherry.
- Amontillado (mostly dry): An aged sherry that takes on nutty flavors of peanuts and butter.
- Oloroso (dry): A very aged and dark sherry with higher alcohol content.
- Cream Sherry (sweet): Made by blending Oloroso with Pedro Ximenez Sherry.
- Moscatel (sweet): A sweet sherry with fig and date flavors.
- Pedro Ximenez (PX) (very sweet): A very sweet sherry with brown sugar and figlike flavors.

### Madeira

Madeira is produced on the island of Madeira. The wines undergo a heating and oxidation process. The result is a rich fortified wine with walnut-like flavors, salinity and an oiliness on the palate.

Styles:
- Sercial (dry): The driest and lightest of all the grapes in Madeira.
- Verdelho (dry): Has citrus notes and develops nutty flavors of almond and walnut with time.
- Bual (sweet): Leans on the sweet side with notes of burnt caramel, brown sugar, fig, rootbeer and black walnut.
- Malmsey (sweet): Has orange citrus notes and caramel to their taste along with the oily oxidized nutty flavor.

### Vin Doux Naturel (VDN)

Vin Doux Naturel are made in a similar style to Port where a base wine is created and finished with neutral grape brandy.

- Grenache-based VDN: Typically from the south of France, such as Maury, Rasteau and Banyuls.
- Muscat-based VDN: Muscat de Rivesaltes, Muscat de Beaumes de Venise, Rutherglen Muscat (Australia).
- Malvasia-based VDN: Mostly from Italy and Sicily.
- Mavrodaphni: From Greece, a sweet red wine with many similarities to Port.

## Dessert Wine Flavors and Characteristics

Dessert wines have residual sugar that creates their characteristic sweetness. Production methods include:
- Late harvest: grapes left on the vine to concentrate sugars
- Noble rot (botrytis): fungus concentrates sugars and adds complexity
- Straw wine (passito): grapes dried on mats to raisinate
- Ice wine (eiswein): grapes frozen on the vine
- Fortification: adding brandy to stop fermentation

## Serving Dessert Wine

Dessert wines are best served in small glasses, typically 2-3 oz pours. Serving temperature varies by style:
- Sparkling dessert wines: well chilled, around 45F
- Light sweet whites: chilled, around 45-50F
- Rich sweet whites (Sauternes, Tokaji): slightly cool, around 50-55F
- Fortified wines (Port, Sherry): room temperature or slightly cool

## Dessert Wine and Food Pairings

The key rule: wine should be at least as sweet as the dessert, or sweeter. Classic pairings:
- Sauternes with foie gras or creme brulee
- Port with chocolate
- Tokaji with rich cheese or panettone
- Moscato d'Asti with fresh fruit and light pastries
- PX sherry poured over vanilla ice cream
- Late harvest Riesling with apple tarts or pumpkin pie
- Vin Santo with biscotti (traditional Tuscan pairing)
