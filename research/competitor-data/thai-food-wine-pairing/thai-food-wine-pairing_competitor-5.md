# Wine to Pair with Thai Food

## Episode Overview

This episode of The Wine Pair Podcast explores wine pairings for Thai cuisine, addressing why this pairing challenge is particularly complex and featuring three wines under $20.

## Why Thai Food and Wine Pairing Is Difficult

The hosts identify several challenges:

- Diverse Thai dishes: Beyond Pad Thai, options include Tom Kha Gai, curries, satays, spring rolls, and Thai omelets
- Complex flavor profiles: Fresh herbs like lemongrass and Thai basil combine with spices, coconut milk, lime, and turmeric
- Heat elements: Chili peppers, black pepper, garlic, and ginger complicate pairings
- Varied proteins: Chicken, pork, shrimp, and fish appear throughout the cuisine

## Wine and Food Pairing Philosophy

The hosts debate whether "correct" pairings exist. They acknowledge that while some people find pairing concepts pretentious or overly restrictive, they believe pairing can genuinely enhance both wine and food experiences without requiring one perfect match.

## Recommended Wine Styles for Thai Food

Best options include:
- Crisp whites (Sauvignon Blanc, Pinot Grigios, Chenin Blanc, dry Rieslings)
- Sparkling wines (Brut style with acidity)
- Off-dry whites (Gewürztraminer, semi-sweet Riesling)
- Light reds (Gamay, Pinot Noir, chilled)
- Dry rosés (Provence-style)

Avoid: High-tannin, heavily oaked, high-alcohol wines that overpower delicate spice profiles

## Wines Reviewed

**1. Radley & Finch Alley Pack Chenin Blanc (2022)**
- Region: South Africa
- Price: $10.99
- Ratings: Joe 8/10, Carmela 7/10
- Notes: Honey, apple, pear with tart finish
- Pairing: Thai curry, Pad Thai, spring rolls

**2. Maison Angelot Bugey Gamay (2022)**
- Region: Savoie, France
- Price: $14.99
- Ratings: Joe 7/10, Carmela 7/10
- Notes: Fruity with strawberry and cherry notes, low tannin
- Pairing: Pad Thai, fried rice, spicy curry

**3. Henri Bourgeois Le Vert Galant Pineau d'Aunis Rosé (2022)**
- Region: Loire Valley, France
- Price: $18.99
- Ratings: Joe 6/10, Carmela 6/10
- Notes: Strawberry, watermelon, slight bitterness
- Pairing: High-heat curries

## About Pineau d'Aunis

This lesser-known Loire Valley grape (pronounced "Pinot Dough-Neece") is genetically unrelated to Pinot Noir or Chenin Blanc despite similar naming. The rosé version offers a lighter alternative to its red expression, featuring savory herbal notes alongside red fruit flavors.
