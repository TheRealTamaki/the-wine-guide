# The Absolute Best Wines To Pair With Thai Food

By James Hastings | Jan. 30, 2025

## Overview

Thai cuisine presents unique pairing challenges due to its complex blend of spicy, fragrant, and aromatic flavors. The article explores two wine pairing strategies: matching contrasting characteristics or amplifying similar flavor notes. Based on the author's experience in hospitality and living in Thailand, here are the recommended wines.

## White Wine Pairings

### Riesling
Aromatic white wine from Germany with stone fruit and citrus notes. High acidity cuts through rich dishes, while optional sweetness tempers spice. Best paired with Tom Yum Goong (hot and sour shrimp soup) or papaya salad.

### Sauvignon Blanc
Typically dry with fruit notes ranging from green apple to tropical flavors. Herbaceous varieties work well with fragrant dishes like Pad Krapow (holy basil stir fry).

### Pinot Gris
Versatile wine with medium acidity and body. Stone and orchard fruit flavors complement Thai green curry and creamy Tom Kha soup.

### Chenin Blanc
Loire Valley wine combining "inherent sweetness and high acidity," ideal for oilier Thai dishes like Pad See Ew noodles and fried rice.

### Gewürztraminer
Characteristically sweet with fragrant lychee and rose petal notes plus spice elements. Pairs excellently with Massaman curry or rich meats.

### Grüner Veltliner
Austrian wine with tangy citrus and peppery herbaceousness. Excellent with Thai vegetable dishes and crispy proteins.

### Torrontés
Argentine white wine with "fragrant notes of orange blossom and rose petals" combined with stone fruit. Best with seafood dishes like steamed fish or crab cakes.

## Rosé Options

### Dry Rosé
Light-bodied, fragrant varieties work with grilled Thai foods and aromatic salads like larb.

### Sparkling Rosé
Effervescent option that cleanses the palate between courses and pairs well with fried appetizers.

## Red Wine Pairings

### Pinot Noir
Lighter-bodied red with red fruit notes. Works with darker meats and red curry dishes without overpowering spice.

### Beaujolais
Minimally tannic, relatively acidic red made with Gamay grapes. Often chilled to enhance fruity notes and reduce heat. Pairs well with pork dishes and northern Thai sausages.

### Zinfandel
Moderately tannic California wine with jammy fruit and subtle sweetness. Suitable for less complex Thai curries and grilled meats.

## Sparkling Options

### Prosecco
Italian sparkling wine that's sweeter and fruitier than champagne. Excellent palate cleanser for fried foods and pairs well with Thai desserts like mango sticky rice.

**Key Takeaway**: The article demonstrates that successful Thai wine pairing depends on matching acidity levels, managing spice through sweetness, and choosing wines whose aromatic qualities complement the cuisine's complex flavor profiles.
