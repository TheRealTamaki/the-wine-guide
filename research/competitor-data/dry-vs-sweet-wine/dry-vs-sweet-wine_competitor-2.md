# Wines Listed from Dry to Sweet (Charts)

Any wine, be it Riesling or Cabernet, can be either dry or sweet. The winemaker determines its sweetness. Popular varietal wines and styles tend to share the same sweetness level. Wine sweetness ranges from virtually nothing to upwards of 70% sweetness.

## Sweetness Levels

When reading a tech sheet:

- Below 1% sweetness, wines are considered dry.
- Above 3% sweetness, wines taste off-dry or semi-sweet.
- Wines above 5% sweetness are noticeably sweet.
- Dessert wines start at around 7-9% sweetness.
- 1% sweetness equals 10 g/L residual sugar (RS).
- 1% sweetness is a little less than two carbs per 5 oz serving (150 ml).

The average wine drinker cannot detect sweetness levels below 1.5%. That said, trained tasters can guesstimate sweetness within about 0.2%.

## Where Does the Sweetness in Wine Come From?

Thousands of years ago, winemakers figured out how to stop fermentation by various means, resulting in leftover grape sugars. Wine geeks call these leftover sugars residual sugar. There are some poor-quality sweet wines that add sugar after fermentation to make them sweeter, but this is generally frowned upon. The exception to this rule is sparkling wine.

We are not particularly adept at sensing sweetness. For example, bitterness, or tannins in wine, reduces the perception of sugar. So does acidity.

## Red Wine Sweetness Levels (Dry to Sweet)

Dry red wines include Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, Pinot Noir, Syrah/Shiraz, Zinfandel, Malbec, Barbera, Sangiovese, Tempranillo, Cabernet Franc.

Off-dry red wines include some styles of Grenache, Lambrusco.

Sweet red wines include Port, Banyuls, Recioto della Valpolicella, Tawny Port.

## White Wine Sweetness Levels (Dry to Sweet)

Bone-dry white wines: Muscadet, Gruner Veltliner, Sauvignon Blanc, Pinot Grigio (Italian style).

Dry white wines: Chardonnay, Viognier, White Burgundy, Dry Riesling, Albarino, Dry Gewurztraminer.

Off-dry white wines: German Riesling Kabinett, Chenin Blanc (off-dry styles), Pinot Gris (Alsatian), Gewurztraminer (off-dry).

Sweet white wines: Riesling Spatlese/Auslese, Moscato d'Asti, Vouvray Demi-Sec.

Dessert wines: Sauternes, Tokaji Aszu, Late Harvest Riesling, Ice Wine, Pedro Ximenez Sherry.

## Key Concepts

Residual sugar is the sugar remaining in wine after fermentation. Dry wines have minimal residual sugar. Sweet wines retain more residual sugar.

Tannins reduce the perception of sweetness. Acidity also reduces the perception of sweetness. A high-acid wine can taste drier than its actual sugar level suggests.

Fruity flavors and sweetness are different things. A Pinot Noir can have abundant cherry and raspberry aromas while being bone dry. The fruit character comes from aromatic compounds, not sugar.

Alcohol levels tend to be higher in dry wines because more sugar was converted to alcohol during fermentation.

## Sparkling Wine Sweetness Scale

Sparkling wines use a specific sweetness scale:
- Brut Nature / Extra Brut: bone dry (less than 3 g/L RS)
- Brut: dry (less than 12 g/L RS)
- Extra Dry / Extra Sec: off-dry (12-17 g/L RS)
- Sec: semi-sweet (17-32 g/L RS)
- Demi-Sec: sweet (32-50 g/L RS)
- Doux: very sweet (50+ g/L RS)
