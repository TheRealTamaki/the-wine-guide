# The Wine Pairing Guide to Grilling and Barbecue

## Overview

This comprehensive guide from Vindulge explores how to pair wines with grilled and barbecued foods. The author emphasizes that while specific recommendations exist, "drink what you like" remains the ultimate rule of wine pairing.

## Key Pairing Principles

The approach focuses on two main strategies:
- **Complementing**: matching similar characteristics like weight, intensity, and acidity
- **Contrasting**: pairing opposing flavors such as sweet with spicy

The author stresses that "use your rubs, sauces, and glazes to dictate the wine, not the specific protein (in most cases)."

## Grilling vs. Barbecue

**Grilling** involves cooking food directly over hot fire using "hot and fast" methods, while **Barbecue** (BBQ) cooks food indirectly with smoke at low temperatures (250°F or less). This distinction matters because smoke and sugar in barbecue can interfere with wine pairings.

## Regional BBQ Styles and Wine Pairings

### Carolina Style
- **Characteristic**: Vinegar-based sauce with thin consistency and high acidity
- **Wine pairing**: Barbera, Sangiovese, Monastrell, or acidic Riesling

### South Carolina Style
- **Characteristic**: Mustard-laden sauce with spice elements
- **Wine pairing**: Syrah, Zinfandel, or off-dry Riesling with 1-2% residual sugar

### Memphis Style
- **Characteristic**: Dry rub with minimal sauce (ketchup-based, served aside)
- **Wine pairing**: Washington Merlot, Lambrusco, or dry rosé

### Kansas City Style
- **Characteristic**: Thick, ketchup-based sauce covering proteins heavily
- **Wine pairing**: Zinfandel or New World Syrah for assertive characteristics

### Texas Style
- **Characteristic**: Dry rub and smoke focus (especially brisket), minimal sauce
- **Wine pairing**: Medium to full-bodied reds like Tempranillo, Rhone Syrah, or GSM blends

## General BBQ Wine Pairing Guidelines

Key considerations include:
- Smoke can overpower delicate wines, so choose bold options
- "Barbecue, when done right, shouldn't be a smoke bomb"
- Avoid delicate wines when smoking; they're acceptable for grilling
- Big bold flavors require big bold wines

## Grilled Food Pairings

### Sparkling Wine
Versatile with seafood, vegetables, and chicken. Cava and Prosecco work for everyday options.

### White Wines
- **Chardonnay**: grilled chicken
- **Riesling**: poultry and fish
- **Pinot Gris**: grilled fruit and seafood
- **Albariño**: grilled seafood

### Rosé
Offers versatility across light dishes to beef and pork, with acidity that refreshes.

### Red Wines
- **Malbec**: standard for grilled beef
- **Cabernet Sauvignon/Merlot**: steaks
- **Zinfandel/Syrah**: BBQ sauce-covered proteins
- **Pinot Noir**: salmon and pork dishes

## Final Recommendations

The author's personal preference is dry rosé for summer grilling, emphasizing that sparkling wine pairs with "EVERYTHING." The ultimate philosophy: choose wines that make you happy, as proper pairings should enhance rather than constrain the dining experience.
