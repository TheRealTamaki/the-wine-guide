# 3 Perfect Wine Pairings for Sushi

**By Kyle Thacker**
*Jul 31, 2019*

## Introduction

Sushi has gained tremendous popularity across the United States as diners become more adventurous. Rather than representing a single dish, sushi is fundamentally a preparation style. Its versatility comes from the wide range of ingredients and flavor combinations that can be incorporated, allowing restaurants to cater to diverse palates. However, this very diversity presents challenges when selecting wine pairings. This article explores three excellent wine options to complement your sushi meal.

## Vinho Verde

Vinho Verde is not a specific grape variety but rather a category encompassing multiple wine styles, red, white, rosé, and beyond. For sushi pairing, white Vinho Verde is the recommended choice.

The name translates to "young wine." These wines are bottled early and undergo natural fermentation in the bottle, creating "slight carbonation and a refreshing effervescence" similar to subdued sparkling wine. Look for fruity, floral white versions resembling Pinot Grigio.

The carbonation provides subtle palate-cleansing properties while green fruit notes pair delicately with seafood. The bubbles effectively cut through heavier sushi elements like soy sauce or tempura batter.

**Recommendation:** Broadbent offers sparkling bright citrus notes.

## Dry Rosé

Rosé wines, whether sparkling or still, complement seafood exceptionally well. A balanced dry rosé withstands the flavor and texture complexity that sushi presents without overwhelming delicate fish.

French rosés from Alsace and Burgundy regions, made from pinot noir grapes, are ideal. These wines balance "reserved summer fruit flavors like strawberry and cherry, brightened by acidity with a dry finish," providing sophistication without excessive sweetness.

**Recommendation:** Gustave Lorantz le Rosé features light summer berry fruits and bright minerals.

## Red Burgundy

Pinot noir from Burgundy may seem surprising but works remarkably well with sushi. Burgundy produces exclusively pinot noir for red wines and chardonnay for whites. These French pinots differ markedly from California and New Zealand versions, with greater acidity and lighter profiles.

Burgundy's limestone soil imparts minerality that balances the wine's red fruit characteristics. This combination enables the wine to "stand up to bold flavors found in sushi" while its acidity cuts through oily fish. The restrained style won't overpower delicate white fish.

Burgundy wines are classified hierarchically: Grand Cru (best quality), Premiere Cru, Village Wines, and Regional Wines. Regional Wines, though the lowest tier, maintain quality and aging potential.

**Recommendation:** Saint-Romain "Sous le Chateau" offers red fruits and tannins.

## Alternative Pairings

### Beer
Carbonation and yeast-forward profiles make beer an excellent sushi companion. Lagers like Kirin Ichiban work particularly well.

### Sake
While sake isn't traditionally paired with sushi in Japan because "the rice wine doesn't compliment the rice dish," modern pairings prove highly compatible. Try Funaguchi Kikusui, an un-pasteurized, un-diluted canned option.

### Green Tea
For alcohol-free options maintaining tradition, green tea provides nutty and herbal notes that pair wonderfully with sushi. Konacha ("agari" green tea) is the traditional choice served at sushi restaurants.
