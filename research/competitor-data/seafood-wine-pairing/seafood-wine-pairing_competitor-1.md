# A Basic Guide to Wine and Seafood Pairings

**Published:** February 5, 2022
**Author:** Guest User

## Introduction

The author shares their journey into wine appreciation after turning 21, combining this interest with knowledge gained through an internship at Eating with the Ecosystem. They acknowledge their beginner-level expertise and emphasize that "this article is solely meant for those who are also twenty one years and older."

The guide organizes wine into three categories—white, red, and sparkling—with recommended local seafood pairings for each.

---

## White Wines

White wines are the traditional pairing with seafood due to their light character matching lighter proteins. The author notes the principle: "what grows together goes together," as white wines are typically grown in coastal regions alongside seafood sources.

### Chardonnay

This versatile option works as a "one size fits all" wine for seafood. Recommended pairings include monkfish, lobster, jonah crab, haddock, cod, pollock, hake, and redfish.

### Sauvignon Blanc

High acidity makes this bright wine ideal for fattier fish like mackerel, butterfish, and bluefish, as well as white flaky varieties and shellfish such as lobster, quahogs, and mussels.

### Pinot Grigio

Its floral, fruity profile suits lighter fish including flounder, John dory, whiting, scup, and shellfish like crab and clams.

---

## Sparkling Wines

Though pricier, these carbonated options pair excellently with raw bars and shellfish.

### Champagne

Light and crisp with adjustable sweetness levels, champagne complements oysters and other shellfish preparations.

### Prosecco

More budget-friendly than champagne, this lighter option works well with oysters, lobster hors d'oeuvres, and steamed clams.

### Sparkling Rosé

Fruity with higher acidity, this wine pairs nicely with oily fish and shellfish.

---

## Red Wines

Red wines present greater pairing challenges with seafood due to their heavier profiles. The rule: prepare seafood with heartier ingredients when using red wines.

### Merlot

The lightest red option, merlot works best with fatty fish like bluefish and tuna, especially when prepared in stews or heavier dishes.

### Pinot Noir

Moderately dry with balanced acidity, this accessible option suits both lean and oily fish including monkfish, halibut, scup, and mackerel.

---

## Practical Application

The author demonstrates the guide through personal experience, preparing spaghetti with littleneck clams using chardonnay. They describe the dish as "light" while the wine's "oakiness was a bit strong," suggesting sauvignon blanc might offer better balance in future attempts.

---

## Key Takeaway

The author emphasizes: "There is no right or wrong way to pair seafood and wine, so in the end, drink what you want!" They encourage readers to experiment with local seafood varieties while consulting Eating with the Ecosystem's recipe collection for inspiration.
