# How to pair wines with chicken

Chicken can be roasted, grilled, fried, poached, cooked in casseroles, tagines, salads, stir-fries, skewers, and Sunday lunch dishes. Wine pairing depends on how the chicken is cooked and served. White wine with chicken, especially Chardonnay, is a classic match, but rose, orange wine, and lighter red wines can also work.

Darker rose styles tend to have more tannin and structure, which makes them useful with food. Light red wines with good acidity, such as Pinot Noir, Gamay, and Garnacha, can work well with chicken dishes and can even be served chilled. Some richer chicken dishes, such as casseroles and chicken pie, can handle reds with a little more body.

The basic principle is to avoid overpowering the meat or dish. A wine with ripe fruit and decent acidity can work with richer chicken dishes, while too much tannin can mask the flavors.

### Roast chicken

Roast chicken is often cooked with herbs, garlic, lemon, butter, or olive oil. Chardonnay is a classic match, especially an oaked style with more palate weight or a New World Chardonnay with riper fruit.

In summer, roast chicken served at room temperature with gratin or salad can work with Provence rose. Roast chicken with lemon, herbs, and garlic can also work with Austrian Gruner Veltliner, aromatic white wine, or full-flavored rose because acidity and herbs need freshness.

Roast chicken with morel mushrooms can work with vin jaune from the Jura, white Burgundy, or cool-climate Chardonnay. Similar white Burgundy styles can also work with chicken pie.

### Chicken in Asian cuisine

Chicken appears in many Asian cuisines, including Thai, Malaysian, Japanese, and Chinese dishes. These dishes can be difficult to pair because of complex herbs and spices.

Aromatic white wines such as Riesling and Gewurztraminer can complement complex Asian flavors. Alsace, New Zealand, and Torrontes from Argentina are useful places to look. Aromatic whites can also pair with minced chicken dim sum or sesame chicken. For spicy dishes such as Thai chicken curry, off-dry Riesling with residual sugar is a reliable choice.

### Fried chicken with Champagne

Fried chicken needs wine with enough acidity to cut through the fattiness of the frying. Sparkling wines with high acidity and freshness are ideal. Champagne, Cava, Cap Classique, English sparkling wine, and Tasmanian sparkling wine can all work. Lighter Prosecco styles may not have enough body for bigger fried chicken flavors.

### Styles to consider when matching wines with chicken

- Chardonnay
- Gruner Veltliner
- Riesling
- Champagne
- Pinot Noir
- Beaujolais
- Spanish Garnacha

### Coq au vin and chicken casserole dishes

Chicken casserole and coq au vin can work with red wine. Pinot Noir is the classic choice, especially Burgundy. Cool-climate New World Pinot Noir from Chile, Mornington Peninsula, Oregon, New Zealand, or California's Santa Barbara County can also work.

In general, fruit-forward wines with low tannins and good acidity help lift chicken dishes. Regional flavors can also guide the match. Italian chicken with pesto points toward Vermentino. Spanish-style chicken with sweet or smoky paprika can work with Garnacha.
